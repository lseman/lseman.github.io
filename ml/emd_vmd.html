Why do we need adaptive decompositions?

Real-world signals are nonstationary, nonlinear, and multi-scale.

Traditional Fourier or wavelet methods impose fixed bases. Many signals instead contain components whose frequency and amplitude change over time — speech, machinery vibration, ocean waves, EEG rhythms.

Empirical mode decomposition builds the basis from the signal itself. The modes adapt to local oscillations instead of forcing the data into a predetermined dictionary.

Typical challenges

  • Time-varying frequency — chirps, decays, and transient oscillations.
  • Amplitude modulation — bursts, envelopes, and beat phenomena.
  • Mode mixing — multiple oscillations interfering in the same spectral band.
  • Noise — measurement noise and environmental interference.

II · EMD basics
04 / 17
What is an intrinsic mode function?

EMD decomposes a signal into adaptive oscillatory modes plus a residue.

An intrinsic mode function (IMF) satisfies two conditions: the number of extrema and zero crossings differ by at most one, and the local mean is zero at every point. These conditions make the IMF locally symmetric and well-suited for instantaneous frequency analysis.

EMD decomposition $$x(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} c_k(t) + r(t),$$ where each $c_k(t)$ is an IMF and $r(t)$ is a monotonic residue.
The modes are derived from the data itself, not from sinusoids. That makes EMD especially powerful for signals with evolving frequency and amplitude.

IMF interpretation

  • $c_1$ captures the fastest oscillation.
  • Higher modes capture progressively slower components.
  • Residue contains trend and non-oscillatory structure.
Why it matters

IMFs provide a nearly orthogonal, adaptive representation that preserves local energy and makes the Hilbert transform meaningful for instantaneous frequency analysis.

What does an intrinsic mode function look like?

An IMF is a locally symmetric oscillatory component.

IMFs behave like narrowband waves whose instantaneous amplitude and frequency can change slowly over time. They are not sinusoids with fixed frequency, but they are locally balanced around zero.

IMF conditions

  • Equal or nearly equal numbers of extrema and zero crossings.
  • Local mean of the upper and lower envelopes is approximately zero.
The IMF definition is intentionally simple: it captures the idea of a single oscillatory mode without imposing a global basis.

III · EMD algorithm
06 / 17
The sifting process

EMD extracts modes with repeated envelope averaging.

Algorithm 1 · Basic EMD
1input: signal x(t), tolerance \epsilon
2set r(t) ← x(t), k ← 1
3while r(t) has more than 1 extremum:
4   set h(t) ← r(t)
5   repeat
6     extract upper and lower envelopes of h(t)
7     compute mean envelope m(t)
8     h(t) ← h(t) - m(t)
9   until h(t) is IMF-like
10   c_k(t) ← h(t)
11   r(t) ← r(t) - c_k(t)
12   k ← k + 1
13end while
14return modes c_k(t) and residue r(t)

Practical decisions

  • Envelope interpolation: cubic spline is common.
  • Stopping criterion: standard deviation or maximum iteration count.
  • End effects: mirror or predictive boundary handling matters.
EMD is empirical: the algorithm is defined by the data-driven sifting loop, not by a single closed-form transform.

III · Sifting walkthrough
07 / 17
One sift in slow motion

EMD builds an IMF by correcting the residual’s local mean, one pass at a time.

Step 1 / 4

Use the controls to walk through one sifting cycle. This is the part of EMD that usually feels abstract until you see the corrections happen in order.

Step 1 · Observe the residual

Mark extrema before trying to extract a mode.

EMD starts from the current residual $r(t)$. The first task is not spectral analysis, but geometry: find local maxima, minima, and zero crossings. Those landmarks determine the envelopes.

  • The densest oscillations usually dominate the first IMF.
  • Extrema location matters more than a global frequency fit.
  • Bad endpoints already create trouble here.
Step 2 · Build the envelopes

Interpolate the maxima and minima separately.

A spline through maxima forms the upper envelope; a spline through minima forms the lower envelope. Together they encode how far the candidate mode is from local symmetry.

  • Cubic splines are common because they are smooth.
  • Envelope quality depends on extrema spacing.
  • Boundary handling influences the first and last cycles.
Step 3 · Estimate the local mean

The average of the envelopes reveals local bias.

Compute $m(t) = \tfrac{e_{\max}(t) + e_{\min}(t)}{2}$. If this mean is not close to zero, then the current waveform is still carrying trend or neighboring-scale contamination.

  • Large mean means the candidate is not IMF-like yet.
  • Subtracting the mean centers the oscillation locally.
  • This is why EMD feels adaptive rather than spectral.
Step 4 · Subtract and test again

After subtraction, we either accept the IMF or sift again.

Set $h(t) \leftarrow h(t) - m(t)$. If the new waveform now satisfies the IMF conditions, keep it as a mode. If not, repeat the same cycle until the local mean is small enough.

  • The inner loop makes one IMF.
  • The outer loop repeats on the remaining residual.
  • Too many sifts can oversmooth the mode.

IV · EMD properties

What makes EMD special?

Local adaptivity, instantaneous frequency, and a data-driven basis.

EMD constructs modes that reflect local oscillation patterns. This gives it an edge on signals with multiple overlapping frequencies and changing envelopes.

data-adaptive nonlinear mode-wise

Instantaneous frequency

Each IMF can be transformed by the Hilbert transform to yield an instantaneous frequency curve $\omega_k(t)$.

Key behavior
  • Modes are ordered by decreasing frequency.
  • Components are local and can vary in amplitude and frequency.
  • The residue often captures trend or low-frequency drift.
The decomposition is not unique in the same way as Fourier; it depends on how splines and stopping rules are chosen. That flexibility is also its strength.

V · Hilbert Transform
From modes to instantaneous frequency

The Hilbert transform turns each IMF into a time-varying amplitude and frequency.

Given an IMF $c_k(t)$, its Hilbert transform $\mathcal{H}[c_k](t)$ is the analytic signal's imaginary part. Together they define instantaneous amplitude and instantaneous frequency.

Analytic signal $$z_k(t) = c_k(t) + j\,\mathcal{H}[c_k](t) = A_k(t)\,e^{j\theta_k(t)}$$
Instantaneous quantities $$A_k(t) = \sqrt{c_k^2(t) + \mathcal{H}[c_k]^2(t)}, \qquad \omega_k(t) = \frac{d\theta_k}{dt}$$
$\omega_k(t)$ is only physically meaningful when $c_k$ is an IMF — nearly mono-component and locally symmetric. This is why the IMF conditions matter.
Geometric picture

Think of the analytic signal as a rotating phasor in the complex plane. Its radius is $A_k(t)$ (the envelope) and its angular velocity is $\omega_k(t)$ (the instantaneous frequency).

HHT = EMD + Hilbert

Applying the Hilbert transform to each IMF and plotting $A_k(t)$ vs $\omega_k(t)$ over time gives the Hilbert-Huang spectrum — a fully adaptive time-frequency representation.

§V · Hilbert Transform EMD & VMD
V · HHT Spectrum
Time-frequency representation

The Hilbert-Huang spectrum plots instantaneous amplitude over time and frequency.

Unlike the short-time Fourier transform, the HHT spectrum has no fixed window. Frequency resolution and time resolution are both local — they follow the signal's own oscillatory structure.

Marginal spectrum $$h(\omega) = \int_0^T A_k(t)\,\delta\!\left(\omega - \omega_k(t)\right) dt$$
Integrating over time gives energy distribution — analogous to a power spectrum but without stationarity assumption.
HHT advantage

No trade-off between time and frequency resolution (no Heisenberg uncertainty in the Fourier sense).

HHT limitation

Not a linear transform — superposition does not hold. Results depend on EMD quality.

Each track shows how one mode's instantaneous frequency evolves over time — something a fixed-basis spectrogram cannot capture directly.

VII · Limitations
When standard EMD struggles

Mode mixing, noise sensitivity, and endpoint effects are real.

1
Mode mixing. One IMF may contain energy from widely separated frequencies, or the same frequency may appear in multiple IMFs.
2
Noise sensitivity. Small perturbations can change the envelope shapes and the resulting IMFs.
3
Boundary effects. Spline envelopes are unstable at the ends, leading to distortion near the signal boundaries.

Empirical caution

The algorithm is powerful, but it is also heuristic. Added noise and ensemble averaging can improve robustness without changing the core adaptive idea.

What it does well

Good for signals with clear oscillatory structure, where the notion of local extrema is meaningful and the component frequencies are sufficiently separated.

VIII · CEEMDAN
Noise-assisted EMD

CEEMDAN stabilizes mode decomposition with ensembles.

Complete Ensemble Empirical Mode Decomposition with Adaptive Noise (CEEMDAN) adds white noise to the signal multiple times, decomposes each perturbed realization, and averages the resulting modes. This reduces mode mixing and improves repeatability.

CEEMDAN mode estimate $$c_k(t) \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \mathrm{EMD}\bigl(x(t) + \sigma_j n_j(t)\bigr)_k,$$ where $n_j(t)$ is white noise and $\sigma_j$ scales the perturbation.
Adaptive noise makes the decomposition less sensitive to the idiosyncratic convexities of a single signal realization, while still preserving the underlying oscillatory content.

CEEMDAN advantages

  • Reduces mode mixing.
  • Produces more stable, repeatable IMFs.
  • Retains adaptivity while adding statistical robustness.

Practical note

The decomposition cost increases with the ensemble size, but the improvement in mode quality is often worth it for noisy or complex signals.

VIII · CEEMDAN Algorithm
The complete ensemble procedure

CEEMDAN adds adaptive noise at each decomposition stage, not just the first.

Algorithm 2 · CEEMDAN
1input: signal x(t), ensemble size N, noise std ε
2for j = 1…N: add noise x_j(t) = x(t) + ε·w_j(t)
3compute first IMF of each x_j via EMD
4c̃₁(t) ← mean over ensemble // stage 1 IMF
5r₁(t) ← x(t) − c̃₁(t) // first residue
6for k = 2, 3, … until stopping:
7   for j = 1…N:
8     compute 1st IMF of r_{k-1}(t) + ε·E_{k-1}(w_j(t))
9     // E_{k-1}: (k-1)-th EMD mode of unit noise
10   c̃_k(t) ← mean over ensemble
11   r_k(t) ← r_{k-1}(t) − c̃_k(t)
12return {c̃_k(t)} and final residue
Key difference from EEMD

In plain EEMD, noise is added only to x(t) at the start. CEEMDAN adds adaptive noise scaled to each residue stage — this ensures the noise always matches the scale of the component being extracted, eliminating residual noise in the final IMFs.

N
Ensemble size. Typical values: 50–500. Larger N → lower noise floor in the average, but proportionally higher cost.
ε
Noise amplitude. Usually 0.01–0.2 × signal std. Too large distorts modes; too small gives no benefit over plain EMD.
The adaptive noise term $E_{k-1}(w_j)$ ensures each stage gets perturbations at the right scale — this is what distinguishes CEEMDAN from EEMD.

Why it works
The injected noise fills gaps in the time-scale sampling, so different realizations do not get trapped by the same local envelope irregularities.
What cancels
The added white noise is random and averages out across the ensemble.
What remains
Oscillatory structures that recur in nearly every realization survive the averaging and become cleaner.
Interpretation

One noisy decomposition is not more trustworthy than plain EMD. The advantage comes from the ensemble: each run perturbs the envelope geometry in a slightly different way, and the average suppresses the accidental part.

A variational alternative

VMD finds band-limited modes by optimization.

Variational Mode Decomposition (VMD) formulates mode extraction as a constrained optimization: each mode is assumed to be compact around a central frequency, and the algorithm jointly estimates the modes and their center frequencies.

VMD objective $$\min_{\{u_k\},\{\omega_k\}} \sum_{k} \left\| \partial_t \left[ u_k(t)\, e^{-j \omega_k t} \right] \right\|_2^2$$
subject to $\displaystyle\sum_k u_k(t) = x(t)$
VMD is model-based rather than envelope-based. It solves for modes whose analytic signals have minimal bandwidth around estimated center frequencies, so the decomposition is guided by an explicit optimization criterion.

How VMD behaves

  • Modes are obtained simultaneously, not sequentially.
  • Frequency centers $\omega_k$ adapt during optimization.
  • It is more stable than plain EMD when spectral bands overlap.
Mental model

Think of VMD as asking each mode to occupy a compact spectral neighborhood while the full collection still reconstructs the observed signal.

Contrast with EMD

EMD peels modes off by local geometry and repeated sifting. VMD instead solves for all modes together under a bandwidth penalty and a reconstruction constraint.

What the extracted modes mean

Read each VMD mode as a band centered around an estimated frequency.

Band-limited modes around center frequencies
ωk means
the center around which the mode concentrates most of its spectral energy.

Tuning

Choose the number of modes and the bandwidth penalty carefully. Too few modes underfit the signal, while too many can split one physical process into multiple narrowband artifacts.

How to read the result

  • A clean mode should look oscillatory and stay concentrated near one dominant band.
  • If two modes chase the same band, the mode count may be too large.
  • If one mode remains broad and messy, the penalty may be too weak.

X · VMD Tuning
Choosing K and α in practice

VMD's two key hyperparameters control how many modes and how narrow each band is.

K
Number of modes. The only parameter that must be set before running. Inspect the signal's power spectrum: count distinct peaks. Start with that K, then check if any two modes chase the same band (too many) or any mode is spectrally broad (too few).
α
Bandwidth penalty. Controls how compact each mode is around its center frequency. Large α → narrow, tightly band-limited modes. Small α → modes can spread across wider frequency ranges. Typical range: $\alpha \in [100, 5000]$ for normalized signals.
τ
Noise tolerance (Lagrange multiplier step). Set to 0 for exact reconstruction, slightly positive for noise-tolerant decomposition.
K too small

One mode absorbs energy from multiple physical processes — broad, mixed spectral content per mode.

K too large

Spurious modes appear. Two modes overlap the same band with slight frequency jitter.

α too small

Modes bleed into each other's frequency bands. Poor separation even with correct K.

α too large

Modes become over-constrained. Reconstruction error grows; signal energy is left in the residual.

Practical recipe: plot the Fourier spectrum first, choose K from peak count, set α = 2000, then sweep ±50% and compare reconstruction error and mode spectra.

X · Optimization loop
15 / 18
How the optimizer iterates

VMD refines modes and center frequencies together instead of peeling them off one by one.

Step 1 / 4

This loop is the key conceptual contrast with EMD: VMD solves a joint optimization problem, so all modes influence each other during every iteration.

Step 1 · Initialize

Start with a target number of modes and rough centers.

We choose $K$ and initialize approximate center frequencies $\omega_k$. This is the main modeling assumption that VMD asks from the user.

  • Initialization can be uniform or informed by the spectrum.
  • Each mode begins as a rough band assignment.
  • The constraint still requires the modes to sum to $x(t)$.
Step 2 · Update one mode at a time

Estimate each mode while treating the others as known.

In the frequency domain, each mode is updated so that it stays compact around its current center frequency while still helping reconstruct the full signal.

  • This is where the bandwidth penalty acts.
  • Overlapping bands are separated by the optimization.
  • All updates are coupled through the reconstruction constraint.
Step 3 · Re-estimate center frequencies

Move each $\omega_k$ to the energy center of its mode.

After the mode shapes improve, the center frequencies are recomputed from the current spectral energy. This lets the bands drift toward the signal rather than remain fixed.

  • Centers shift if the mode was initialized poorly.
  • Stable centers are a sign that the decomposition is settling.
  • The estimate is data-driven but still bandwidth-aware.
Step 4 · Enforce reconstruction and repeat

The dual update keeps the sum of modes faithful to the data.

A dual variable penalizes mismatch between $\sum_k u_k(t)$ and $x(t)$. When both the mismatch and the mode changes become small, the loop stops.

  • Convergence is monitored numerically, not heuristically.
  • All modes are finalized together.
  • This is why VMD is often steadier than plain EMD.

Choosing the right tool

EMD, CEEMDAN, and VMD each have distinct strengths.

Method Best for Trade-offs
EMD Fast, adaptive decomposition for clean signals. Heuristic, can suffer mode mixing and end effects.
CEEMDAN Noisy or complex signals needing robust IMFs. Higher cost due to ensembles; still empirical.
VMD Signals with overlapping spectral bands and smooth modes. Requires mode count and penalty tuning; more complex optimization.
mode adaptivity noise robustness bandwidth control simultaneous estimation
Reach for EMD when…

you want the fastest first look at a signal and the oscillatory scales are visually distinct.

Reach for CEEMDAN when…

the first EMD result looks unstable, mixed, or overly sensitive to small perturbations.

Reach for VMD when…

you need tighter control of bandwidth, cleaner separation of overlapping bands, or a more optimization-driven formulation.

Summarizing the landscape

Pick the method that matches signal structure and robustness needs.

  • EMD is the default adaptive decomposition for signals with clear local oscillations.
  • CEEMDAN adds statistical stability through noise-assisted ensembles.
  • VMD uses a variational model to estimate band-limited modes simultaneously.
Each method is part of a broader toolkit: use EMD for intuition, CEEMDAN for robustness, and VMD when frequency-band control matters most.

Practical recipe

  • Start with EMD to inspect the mode structure.
  • Use CEEMDAN if mode mixing or noise is apparent.
  • Try VMD when you want a more principled bandwidth-based decomposition.

Implementation notes

Careful boundary handling, stopping criteria, and mode count selection are often more important than small algorithmic differences.
