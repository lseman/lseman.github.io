Time series, before they meet the model.

A practical and theoretical companion to preprocessing temporal data for machine learning — stationarity, decomposition, filtering, causality, and how to know when any of it is working.

For students of DAS410108 Language Python · numpy · statsmodels · scipy Reading time ≈ 45 min

Most failures of time series machine learning are not failures of the model. They are failures of the data that was handed to the model — data that was non-stationary when stationarity was assumed, contaminated when the signal was reachable, or aggregated in a way that destroyed the very thing one wanted to predict.

This guide is a practical map of what happens between raw observations and training tensors. The terrain is not difficult, but it is deceptive: most preprocessing steps look harmless, and most of them silently change what your model is allowed to learn. We will look at each transformation through three lenses — what it does mathematically, how to compute it in Python, and how to tell whether it helped.

If a single sentence had to summarise the philosophy of this document, it would be this: every preprocessing step is a hypothesis about the data-generating process. Differencing assumes a unit root. Detrending assumes a deterministic trend. Filtering assumes that noise and signal live in separable frequency bands. The job is to make those hypotheses explicit, and to falsify them when we can.

§ 01

Anatomy of a time series

A time series is, formally, a realisation of a stochastic process — a family of random variables indexed by time, $\{X_t\}_{t \in \mathcal{T}}$. In practice we observe a finite sample $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_N$ at regular intervals $\Delta t$, and our task is to reason about the process from this single trajectory. That last word matters: unlike most statistics, in time series we typically have one realisation, not many. Asymptotic guarantees come from $N \to \infty$, not from independent replicas.

The classical decomposition assumes the observation can be written as a sum (or product) of unobserved components:

$$ x_t \;=\; T_t \;+\; S_t \;+\; C_t \;+\; \varepsilon_t $$

where $T_t$ is the trend (slow, possibly stochastic drift), $S_t$ is the seasonality (a periodic component with known period $p$), $C_t$ is the cycle (a quasi-periodic component without a fixed period), and $\varepsilon_t$ is the irregular or noise term. The multiplicative variant $x_t = T_t \cdot S_t \cdot C_t \cdot \varepsilon_t$ is appropriate when the amplitude of seasonality scales with the level of the series — a log transform converts it back to additive.

Two more notions belong here. The autocovariance $\gamma(s, t) = \mathrm{Cov}(X_s, X_t)$ measures linear dependence between observations at times $s$ and $t$. When the process is stationary this collapses to a function of the lag alone, $\gamma(h) = \gamma(0, h)$, and we get the autocorrelation function (ACF) $\rho(h) = \gamma(h)/\gamma(0)$. Almost every classical method — ARMA, spectral analysis, Granger causality — is built on top of the autocovariance. 

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf

# Synthesise a series with trend + seasonality + noise
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
N = 500
t = np.arange(N)
trend       = 0.02 * t
seasonal    = 2.0 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 24)        # period 24
noise       = rng.normal(0, 0.5, size=N)
x = trend + seasonal + noise
series = pd.Series(x)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(9, 4))
plot_acf(series, lags=60, ax=ax[0])
plot_pacf(series, lags=60, ax=ax[1], method="ywm")
plt.tight_layout()

The ACF of a series with strong seasonality shows peaks at lags $p, 2p, 3p, \dots$; the ACF of a trending series decays painfully slowly. Reading these plots is a habit worth developing — they tell you, before any model, what kind of structure you are dealing with.

§ 02

Stationarity, and why nearly every method quietly demands it

Stationarity is the assumption that the laws governing the process do not drift through time. There are two flavours of it that you will see in print:

Strict (strong) stationarity. $\{X_t\}$ is strictly stationary if, for all $k$ and all $h$, the joint distribution of $(X_{t_1}, \dots, X_{t_k})$ equals that of $(X_{t_1+h}, \dots, X_{t_k+h})$.

Weak (covariance) stationarity. $\{X_t\}$ is weakly stationary if $\mathbb{E}[X_t] = \mu$ is constant, $\mathrm{Var}(X_t) = \sigma^2 < \infty$ is constant, and $\mathrm{Cov}(X_t, X_{t+h}) = \gamma(h)$ depends only on the lag $h$.

For Gaussian processes the two notions coincide. For everything else, weak stationarity is what we actually test — it is strictly weaker and strictly enough for second-order theory. In what follows "stationary" means "weakly stationary" unless otherwise stated.

Why do we care? Because virtually every classical estimator implicitly relies on it. The sample autocovariance $\hat{\gamma}(h) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_t (x_t - \bar{x})(x_{t+h} - \bar{x})$ converges to $\gamma(h)$ only when $\gamma(s, t)$ depends on lag alone. Estimators of the spectral density (the Fourier transform of the autocovariance) presume the same. ARMA likelihoods, kriging in time, Kalman steady-state covariances — all assume that the past is informative about the future in the same way it was informative about the past.

Deep models — Transformers, RNNs, state-space models — are not magically immune. They are extraordinarily flexible function approximators, but the train/test split, the loss landscape, and the assumption that batches are exchangeable all break under non-stationarity. RevIN, Dish-TS and similar reversible instance normalisations were invented precisely to inject local stationarity at the input and reverse it at the output. They are preprocessing in disguise.

There is one further distinction that causes endless confusion. A series can fail stationarity in two essentially different ways:

  • Trend-stationary — the series equals a deterministic function of time plus a stationary process: $x_t = f(t) + \eta_t$. Subtract $f(t)$ and you are done.
  • Difference-stationary (unit root) — the series contains a stochastic trend, $x_t = x_{t-1} + \eta_t$. Subtraction of a deterministic trend leaves a non-stationary residual; what you need is the difference $\Delta x_t = x_t - x_{t-1}$.

Applying the wrong remedy is more harmful than doing nothing — detrending a unit-root process produces spurious cycles (Nelson and Kang, 1981), and differencing a trend-stationary process inflates noise and removes useful low-frequency information. The diagnostic for which world you are in is a pair of tests, not a single one.

§ 03

The hypothesis-testing arsenal

No single test settles the question. Each is built around a different null hypothesis and has different blind spots; the practical workflow is to triangulate. Here are the four you should know.

Augmented Dickey–Fuller (ADF)

The ADF test estimates the regression

$$ \Delta x_t \;=\; \alpha \;+\; \beta t \;+\; \gamma\, x_{t-1} \;+\; \sum_{i=1}^{p} \delta_i\, \Delta x_{t-i} \;+\; \varepsilon_t $$

and tests the null $H_0: \gamma = 0$ (unit root present) against $H_1: \gamma < 0$ (stationary). The lag order $p$ controls for serial correlation in the residuals. The test statistic is not the usual $t$-statistic — under the null, $\hat{\gamma}$ has a non-standard distribution tabulated by MacKinnon. 

from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller

result = adfuller(series, autolag="AIC", regression="ct")  # ct = constant + trend
adf_stat, p_value, used_lag, n_obs, crit_values, icbest = result

print(f"ADF statistic   : {adf_stat:.4f}")
print(f"p-value         : {p_value:.4f}")
print(f"used lag (AIC)  : {used_lag}")
for level, cv in crit_values.items():
    print(f"  critical {level}: {cv:.4f}")

# Decision rule: reject H0 (unit root) if p < 0.05  ⇒ series is stationary

The regression argument matters more than is usually advertised. Use "n" for no constant (rare; only for centred series), "c" for a constant (most common), "ct" for constant and linear trend (when a deterministic trend is plausible), and "ctt" for quadratic trend. Misspecifying this is the single most common reason ADF gives the "wrong" answer.

KPSS — the test with the opposite null

Kwiatkowski–Phillips–Schmidt–Shin invert the question: the null is stationarity, and the alternative is a unit root. The intuition is that under stationarity the partial sum process behaves like a Brownian bridge, while under a unit root it grows without bound.

$$ \mathrm{KPSS} \;=\; \frac{1}{N^2 \hat{\sigma}^2} \sum_{t=1}^{N} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{t} \hat{e}_i \right)^2 $$

where $\hat{e}_i$ are the residuals from regressing $x_t$ on a constant (and possibly a trend), and $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is a long-run variance estimate. 

from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import kpss

kpss_stat, p_value, lags, crit_values = kpss(series, regression="ct", nlags="auto")
print(f"KPSS statistic : {kpss_stat:.4f}")
print(f"p-value        : {p_value:.4f}")
for level, cv in crit_values.items():
    print(f"  critical {level}: {cv:.4f}")

# Decision rule: reject H0 (stationarity) if p < 0.05  ⇒ series is non-stationary

The mirrored null is what makes the ADF/KPSS combination powerful. The four-way table below is worth memorising.

ADF KPSS Diagnosis
Rejects $H_0$ Fails to reject Stationary. Proceed.
Fails to reject Rejects $H_0$ Non-stationary. Difference or detrend.
Fails to reject Fails to reject Inconclusive. More data, or look at PACF/structural break tests.
Rejects $H_0$ Rejects $H_0$ Trend-stationary. Detrend (don't difference).

Worked example: three series, same tests, different truth

A reliable way to build intuition is to run the same test battery on synthetic series where you control the data-generating process. Below, we generate three cases with equal length and similar noise scale: trend-stationary, unit-root, and seasonal unit-root. The goal is not to chase p-values; it is to map each p-value pattern to a transformation decision. 

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller, kpss

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
N = 600
t = np.arange(N)

# 1) Trend-stationary: deterministic trend + stationary AR(1)-like residual
eta1 = rng.normal(0, 1, N)
e1 = np.zeros(N)
for i in range(1, N):
    e1[i] = 0.6 * e1[i-1] + eta1[i]
x_trend_stationary = 0.01 * t + e1

# 2) Difference-stationary: random walk (unit root)
eps = rng.normal(0, 1, N)
x_unit_root = np.cumsum(eps)

# 3) Seasonal unit root: x_t = x_{t-24} + eps_t
x_seasonal_ur = rng.normal(0, 0.2, N)
for i in range(24, N):
    x_seasonal_ur[i] = x_seasonal_ur[i - 24] + rng.normal(0, 1)

def adf_kpss_pair(x, reg="ct"):
    adf_p = adfuller(x, regression=reg, autolag="AIC")[1]
    kpss_p = kpss(x, regression=reg, nlags="auto")[1]
    return adf_p, kpss_p

rows = []
for name, x in {
    "trend-stationary": x_trend_stationary,
    "unit-root": x_unit_root,
    "seasonal-unit-root": x_seasonal_ur,
}.items():
    adf_p, kpss_p = adf_kpss_pair(x)
    rows.append((name, adf_p, kpss_p))

summary = pd.DataFrame(rows, columns=["series", "adf_p", "kpss_p"])
print(summary)

A practical reading rule for this table: if ADF says "non-stationary" and KPSS says "non-stationary", first suspect a unit root; if ADF says "stationary" but KPSS rejects stationarity, suspect deterministic trend or structural break and test those hypotheses directly.

    Phillips–Perron and DF-GLS

    Phillips–Perron (PP) tests the same null as ADF but uses a non-parametric Newey–West correction for serial correlation and heteroscedasticity rather than including lagged differences. It is more robust to certain forms of heteroscedasticity but less powerful in small samples. DF-GLS (Elliott–Rothenberg–Stock) is a modification of ADF that detrends the series with generalised least squares first, achieving substantially better power against highly persistent alternatives — it is what you should reach for when ADF says "fail to reject" on a series that looks stationary. 

    from arch.unitroot import PhillipsPerron, DFGLS

pp = PhillipsPerron(series, trend="ct")
print(pp.summary().as_text())

dfgls = DFGLS(series, trend="ct", method="AIC")
print(dfgls.summary().as_text())

    Structural breaks: the unspoken assumption

    All of the above assume the data-generating process is constant across the sample. If there was a regime change halfway through — a policy intervention, a sensor recalibration, a market crash — ADF can fail to reject simply because the change makes the series look like a unit root. The fix is to test for breaks first. 

    from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import zivot_andrews
import ruptures as rpt

# Zivot–Andrews: unit root test allowing for ONE endogenous break
za_stat, p_value, crit, baselag, bestbreak = zivot_andrews(series, regression="ct")
print(f"Zivot–Andrews stat: {za_stat:.4f}, break at index {bestbreak}")

# Multiple change-point detection (model-free)
algo = rpt.Pelt(model="rbf").fit(series.values)
breakpoints = algo.predict(pen=10)
print(f"Detected breakpoints: {breakpoints}")

    Stationarity tests are asymptotic. On series shorter than a few hundred observations their power is poor, and on series with strong autocorrelation the effective sample size is much smaller than $N$. When in doubt, simulate: bootstrap a stationary version of your series and check what fraction of the time the test correctly rejects.

    § 04

    Detecting & removing trend

    Once you have decided the series has a trend, two questions remain: is the trend monotonic (which suggests a deterministic functional form) or stochastic (which suggests differencing), and at what time scale does it operate?

    Mann–Kendall — non-parametric monotonic trend

    The Mann–Kendall test asks whether a series exhibits a monotonic trend without assuming linearity or any particular distribution. The test statistic counts concordant pairs:

    $$ S \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} \sum_{j=i+1}^{N} \mathrm{sgn}(x_j - x_i) $$

    Under the null of no trend, $S$ is approximately normal with mean zero and a known variance (with corrections for ties and seasonality). The Sen's slope estimator — the median of pairwise slopes — is its standard companion for estimating the magnitude. 

    import pymannkendall as mk

result = mk.original_test(series)
print(f"Trend       : {result.trend}")        # 'increasing' / 'decreasing' / 'no trend'
print(f"p-value     : {result.p:.4f}")
print(f"Sen's slope : {result.slope:.6f}")

# For seasonal data use mk.seasonal_test(series, period=24)
# For autocorrelated data use mk.hamed_rao_modification_test(series)

    Polynomial and spline detrending

    If the trend is deterministic, fit a smooth function and subtract. Polynomial fits are simple but extrapolate badly; cubic smoothing splines or LOESS give a flexible, data-driven trend. 

    from scipy.signal import detrend
from statsmodels.nonparametric.smoothers_lowess import lowess

# Linear detrending (zero-mean residual)
x_linear = detrend(x, type="linear")

# LOESS — robust local-regression trend
trend_hat = lowess(x, t, frac=0.15, return_sorted=False)
x_loess   = x - trend_hat

    Differencing — for stochastic trends

    For a unit-root process, the appropriate transformation is differencing. The first difference $\Delta x_t = x_t - x_{t-1}$ removes an $I(1)$ trend; the seasonal difference $\Delta_s x_t = x_t - x_{t-s}$ removes a seasonal unit root. 

    x_diff      = series.diff().dropna()                # first difference
x_seas_diff = series.diff(24).dropna()               # seasonal difference (period 24)
x_log_diff  = np.log(series).diff().dropna()         # log-difference ≈ relative change

    Differencing is a lossy operation: you cannot recover the level from the differences without an initial condition, and the differenced series has $N-1$ observations and inflated short-lag autocorrelation. Difference as little as you must. Over-differencing manifests as a strong negative ACF at lag 1.

    Fractional differencing

    Sometimes $d=1$ is too much and $d=0$ is too little — series can be "long-memory", with autocorrelations decaying as a power law. Fractional differencing (Hosking, 1981; popularised by Marcos López de Prado for financial data) generalises the difference operator to non-integer $d$:

    $$ \Delta^d x_t \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{d}{k} (-1)^k x_{t-k}, \qquad d \in (0,1) $$

    Choose the smallest $d$ such that the differenced series passes ADF — this preserves the most memory while achieving stationarity. Libraries like fracdiff or implementations in mlfinlab handle the infinite-sum truncation.

    § 05

    Decomposition methods

    Decomposition is the inverse problem of recovering the latent components $T_t, S_t, \varepsilon_t$ (and possibly more) from $x_t$ alone. Because the problem is under-determined, every method imposes structural assumptions to make it solvable. The differences between methods are precisely the differences between those assumptions.

    Classical additive / multiplicative

    The oldest method. The trend is estimated by a centred moving average of window equal to the seasonal period, the seasonality is estimated by averaging the de-trended residuals at each phase of the cycle, and the irregular component is whatever is left. Simple, stable, and unable to handle changing seasonality or outliers. 

    from statsmodels.tsa.seasonal import seasonal_decompose

res = seasonal_decompose(series, model="additive", period=24, extrapolate_trend="freq")
res.plot()

    STL — Seasonal–Trend decomposition using Loess

    Cleveland et al. (1990) replace the moving averages with LOESS smoothers, which lets the seasonal component evolve and is far more robust to outliers. STL is the modern default for univariate decomposition. 

    from statsmodels.tsa.seasonal import STL

stl = STL(series, period=24, robust=True, seasonal=13, trend=49).fit()
trend_t   = stl.trend
season_t  = stl.seasonal
resid_t   = stl.resid

    The seasonal and trend parameters control the bandwidth of the two LOESS smoothers (both must be odd integers). The cleanest mental model: increase them for smoother components, decrease them for components that evolve more rapidly.

    X-13ARIMA-SEATS

    The U.S. Census Bureau's industrial-strength decomposition, used for nearly every official economic indicator. It combines RegARIMA pre-adjustment (for outliers, calendar effects, level shifts) with model-based signal extraction. Heavy machinery — appropriate when accuracy matters more than transparency. 

    from statsmodels.tsa.x13 import x13_arima_analysis
# Requires the X-13 binary installed on the system.
result = x13_arima_analysis(series, x12path="/path/to/x13as", outlier=True)

    Empirical Mode Decomposition (EMD) and friends

    Huang et al. (1998) introduced a fully data-adaptive decomposition that makes no stationarity, no linearity, and no basis-function assumption. The series is decomposed into Intrinsic Mode Functions (IMFs) by iteratively identifying local maxima and minima, fitting envelopes, and subtracting the envelope mean. The number and frequency content of IMFs are determined by the data, not specified in advance.

    $$ x_t \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathrm{IMF}_k(t) \;+\; r_K(t) $$

    EMD's strengths are also its weaknesses: it has no underlying mathematical model, it suffers from mode mixing (a single IMF containing oscillations of very different scales) and from end effects. EEMD (Ensemble EMD) adds white noise across many realisations to mitigate mode mixing. CEEMDAN (Complete EEMD with Adaptive Noise) does this more rigorously. VMD (Variational Mode Decomposition) replaces the iterative sifting with a constrained optimisation problem, producing well-separated narrow-band modes:

    $$ \min_{\{u_k\},\{\omega_k\}} \sum_k \left\| \partial_t \big[(\delta(t) + j/\pi t) * u_k(t)\big] e^{-j\omega_k t} \right\|_2^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_k u_k = x $$ 
    from PyEMD import EMD, EEMD, CEEMDAN
from vmdpy import VMD

# EMD — fully adaptive sifting
imfs_emd = EMD().emd(x)

# CEEMDAN — noise-assisted, robust to mode mixing
imfs_ceemdan = CEEMDAN(trials=100).ceemdan(x)

# VMD — choose K modes a priori (here K=5), alpha is bandwidth penalty
u, u_hat, omega = VMD(x, alpha=2000, tau=0.0, K=5, DC=0, init=1, tol=1e-7)
# u[k] is the k-th mode in time domain, omega[-1, k] is its centre frequency
    EMD/CEEMDAN

    Number of modes is data-driven. No frequency-band assumption. Suffers from end effects and (less, with CEEMDAN) mode mixing. Excellent for exploratory analysis of nonstationary signals.

    VMD

    Number of modes $K$ and bandwidth penalty $\alpha$ must be chosen. Modes are narrow-band by construction, so much cleaner spectral separation. Robust to noise. The default for SOTA forecasting pipelines.

    Singular Spectrum Analysis (SSA)

    A non-parametric technique that combines elements of classical time-series analysis, multivariate statistics, dynamical systems and signal processing. The series is embedded into a trajectory matrix via a sliding window of length $L$, the matrix is decomposed via SVD, and components are reconstructed by grouping singular triples corresponding to trend, oscillations, and noise. 

    from pyts.decomposition import SingularSpectrumAnalysis

ssa = SingularSpectrumAnalysis(window_size=48, groups=[[0], [1, 2], [3, 4, 5], "rest"])
components = ssa.fit_transform(x.reshape(1, -1))[0]   # (n_groups, N)

    Wavelet decomposition

    The discrete wavelet transform decomposes a signal into approximation (low-frequency) and detail (high-frequency) coefficients at multiple resolutions. Unlike Fourier, wavelets are localised in time — they capture transients and abrupt changes that sinusoidal bases blur out. We will see them again under filtering.

    § 06

    A taxonomy of filters

    A filter is a transformation $\mathcal{F}: x_t \mapsto y_t$ that aims to attenuate one part of the signal while preserving another. The most useful taxonomy organises filters along three axes: linear vs nonlinear, causal vs non-causal, and fixed vs adaptive. The choice along each axis is dictated by the problem.

    Moving averages — the simplest linear filter

    The simple moving average (SMA), exponentially weighted moving average (EMA) and weighted MA are convolutions with fixed kernels. They are easy to implement and easy to misuse: a centred SMA is non-causal (it peeks at the future) and is therefore unusable for real-time forecasting; an EMA is causal and has memory parameterised by a single smoothing constant. 

    w = 12

sma  = series.rolling(window=w, center=False).mean()           # causal SMA
csma = series.rolling(window=w, center=True ).mean()           # NON-CAUSAL (do not use for forecasting)
ema  = series.ewm(span=w, adjust=False).mean()                 # causal EMA
wma  = series.rolling(w).apply(lambda v: np.average(v, weights=np.arange(1, w+1)))

    Centred moving averages are look-ahead filters: $\hat{y}_t$ depends on $x_{t+1}, \dots, x_{t+w/2}$. Using them as features for a forecasting model produces leakage so subtle that train/test metrics will look excellent and live performance will be catastrophic. The rule is simple: if it is a feature for forecasting, it must be causal.

    Savitzky–Golay — local polynomial smoothing

    Fits a polynomial of degree $p$ to a sliding window of length $w$ via least squares, replacing the centre point with the polynomial value. Preserves higher-order moments (peaks, inflection points) far better than an SMA of the same window length. Classic in spectroscopy and physiological signal processing. 

    from scipy.signal import savgol_filter

y_sg = savgol_filter(x, window_length=21, polyorder=3, mode="interp")

    Butterworth and frequency-domain filters

    If the noise and signal occupy different frequency bands, the cleanest separation is to design a filter with a specific frequency response. The Butterworth filter is maximally flat in the passband (no ripple) at the cost of a slower transition. Chebyshev I/II and elliptic filters trade passband or stopband ripple for a sharper cutoff. 

    from scipy.signal import butter, filtfilt, sosfiltfilt, freqz

fs    = 1.0        # sampling frequency (samples / time unit)
fc    = 0.05       # cutoff (in same units as fs / 2)
order = 4
sos = butter(order, fc, btype="low", fs=fs, output="sos")

# zero-phase filtering: forward + backward → no group delay, NON-CAUSAL
y_zp = sosfiltfilt(sos, x)

# causal forward-only filter (use this for real-time / forecasting features)
from scipy.signal import sosfilt
y_causal = sosfilt(sos, x)

    For bandpass filtering pass a two-element Wn=[low, high] and btype="band". The order of the filter trades sharpness against transient ringing — start with 2–4.

    Hodrick–Prescott

    The HP filter, ubiquitous in macroeconomics, decomposes $x_t$ into a trend $\tau_t$ and a cycle $c_t = x_t - \tau_t$ by penalising the second difference of the trend:

    $$ \min_{\{\tau_t\}} \sum_{t=1}^{N} (x_t - \tau_t)^2 \;+\; \lambda \sum_{t=2}^{N-1} \left[ (\tau_{t+1} - \tau_t) - (\tau_t - \tau_{t-1}) \right]^2 $$

    $\lambda$ controls the smoothness — 1600 is the conventional choice for quarterly data, 14400 for monthly, 100 for annual. The HP filter has been heavily criticised (Hamilton, 2018) for inducing spurious dynamics, but it remains pedagogically useful and widely deployed. 

    from statsmodels.tsa.filters.hp_filter import hpfilter
cycle, trend = hpfilter(series, lamb=1600)

    Kalman filter — state-space estimation

    The Kalman filter is the optimal linear estimator under Gaussian noise for a linear state-space model

    $$ \mathbf{s}_t = \mathbf{F}\mathbf{s}_{t-1} + \mathbf{w}_t, \qquad \mathbf{w}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{Q}) $$ $$ x_t = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{s}_t + v_t, \qquad v_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R) $$

    It is recursive (single-pass, $O(d^3)$ per step for $d$-dimensional state), naturally handles missing observations, gives uncertainty estimates for free, and is causal. Local-level and local-linear-trend models written in state-space form give you adaptive smoothers that recover the moving-average and Holt smoothing as special cases. 

    from statsmodels.tsa.statespace.structural import UnobservedComponents

# Local linear trend + stochastic seasonal: a Kalman-smoothed decomposition
mod = UnobservedComponents(series, level="local linear trend",
                            seasonal=24, stochastic_seasonal=True)
res = mod.fit(disp=False)
res.plot_components(figsize=(9, 6))

    Wavelet denoising

    Decompose, threshold, reconstruct. The signal is projected onto a wavelet basis (Daubechies, symlets, coiflets, …); coefficients below a threshold $\lambda$ are set to zero (hard) or shrunk toward zero (soft); the inverse transform gives a denoised signal. Donoho's universal threshold $\lambda = \sigma\sqrt{2\log N}$ is the standard starting point. 

    import pywt

def wavelet_denoise(x, wavelet="db4", level=4, mode="soft"):
    coeffs = pywt.wavedec(x, wavelet, level=level)
    # Estimate noise sigma from the finest detail coefficients (MAD)
    sigma = np.median(np.abs(coeffs[-1])) / 0.6745
    uthresh = sigma * np.sqrt(2 * np.log(len(x)))
    coeffs[1:] = [pywt.threshold(c, value=uthresh, mode=mode) for c in coeffs[1:]]
    return pywt.waverec(coeffs, wavelet)[: len(x)]

y_wav = wavelet_denoise(x)

    Nonlinear smoothers

    When the noise is non-Gaussian or signal contains sharp edges, linear filters do poorly. The median filter replaces each point with the median of its neighbours — devastating against impulse noise. The Hampel filter generalises this by using a robust MAD-based outlier criterion. The bilateral filter, borrowed from image processing, weights neighbours by both temporal proximity and value similarity, preserving discontinuities. 

    from scipy.signal import medfilt

y_med = medfilt(x, kernel_size=7)

def hampel(x, k=7, n_sigma=3.0):
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float).copy()
    L = 1.4826  # consistency constant for Gaussian
    for i in range(k, len(x) - k):
        window = x[i-k:i+k+1]
        med    = np.median(window)
        mad    = L * np.median(np.abs(window - med))
        if mad and abs(x[i] - med) > n_sigma * mad:
            x[i] = med
    return x

      § 07

      Is my filter any good?

      Filtering is the easiest preprocessing step to do badly and the hardest to know you have done badly. A filter that removes "too much" is indistinguishable from a model that fits a smoother signal — until forecast horizons grow and the smoothness was the only thing your model was learning.

      There is no single best metric. There are several families, each illuminating a different failure mode. The discipline is to pick a small basket — three or four metrics from different families — and to require them to agree.

      Reference-based metrics (when ground truth is available)

      If you have a synthetic ground-truth signal $s_t$ contaminated by additive noise $n_t$ to produce the observation $x_t = s_t + n_t$, and your filter produces $\hat{s}_t$, the following are standard:

      Metric Definition Interpretation
      RMSE $\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum (\hat{s}_t - s_t)^2}$ Average reconstruction error, in original units.
      SNR (in) $10\log_{10}\!\frac{\|s\|^2}{\|n\|^2}$ Input quality. Reference, not result.
      SNR (out) $10\log_{10}\!\frac{\|s\|^2}{\|\hat{s}-s\|^2}$ Output quality. Higher is better.
      SNR gain SNR$_\text{out}$ − SNR$_\text{in}$ How much the filter actually improved things (dB).
      PSNR $10\log_{10}\!\frac{\max(s)^2}{\mathrm{MSE}}$ Image-processing carry-over; useful for bounded signals.
      NRMSE $\mathrm{RMSE} / (\max s - \min s)$ Scale-free reconstruction error.
      $R^2$ $1 - \mathrm{SS}_\text{res}/\mathrm{SS}_\text{tot}$ Fraction of variance recovered. 
      import numpy as np

def snr_db(signal, noise):
    return 10.0 * np.log10(np.sum(signal**2) / np.sum(noise**2))

def evaluate_filter(s_true, x_obs, s_hat):
    err_in   = x_obs - s_true
    err_out  = s_hat - s_true
    metrics  = {
        "RMSE_in"   : np.sqrt(np.mean(err_in**2)),
        "RMSE_out"  : np.sqrt(np.mean(err_out**2)),
        "SNR_in_dB" : snr_db(s_true, err_in),
        "SNR_out_dB": snr_db(s_true, err_out),
        "R2"        : 1 - np.sum(err_out**2) / np.sum((s_true - s_true.mean())**2),
    }
    metrics["SNR_gain_dB"] = metrics["SNR_out_dB"] - metrics["SNR_in_dB"]
    return metrics

      Reference-free metrics (real data)

      In practice you almost never have the clean signal. The next best thing is to interrogate the residual $r_t = x_t - \hat{s}_t$. A well-behaved residual should look like the noise model you assumed.

      • Whiteness — the residual ACF should lie within Bartlett's confidence bands. Use the Ljung–Box test: $H_0$ is "first $h$ autocorrelations are jointly zero". A small p-value means structure is leaking into the residual, which is structure your filter discarded.
      • Spectral flatness (Wiener entropy) — $\mathrm{SF} = \mathrm{geomean}(|X(f)|^2) / \mathrm{arithmean}(|X(f)|^2)$, in $[0,1]$. Close to 1 means white-noise-like spectrum; close to 0 means strongly tonal.
      • Energy preservation — $\|\hat{s}\|^2 / \|x\|^2$. Should be close to 1 minus the expected noise fraction.
      • Spectral coherence — for two simultaneously filtered series, the magnitude-squared coherence at low frequencies should be preserved. 
      from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox
from scipy.signal import welch

def residual_diagnostics(x, s_hat, lags=20, fs=1.0):
    r = x - s_hat
    lb = acorr_ljungbox(r, lags=[lags], return_df=True)
    f, P = welch(r, fs=fs, nperseg=min(256, len(r)//4))

    # Spectral flatness
    P_pos = P[P > 0]
    sf = np.exp(np.mean(np.log(P_pos))) / np.mean(P_pos)

    return {
        "ljungbox_pvalue"    : lb["lb_pvalue"].iloc[0],   # > 0.05 → residual is "white enough"
        "spectral_flatness"  : float(sf),                  # closer to 1.0 = whiter
        "energy_preserved"   : float(np.sum(s_hat**2) / np.sum(x**2)),
        "residual_std"       : float(np.std(r)),
    }

      Edge-preservation and feature-preservation

      For signals with sharp transitions (changepoints, spikes, regime boundaries), aggregate metrics hide local damage. Edge Preservation Index compares the gradient of the filtered signal to that of the reference around known transitions; the structural similarity index (SSIM) ported from imaging looks at local mean, variance, and covariance windows.

      Decomposition-specific metrics

      For EMD/VMD-style decompositions, ordinary error metrics miss the actual failure mode, which is mode mixing. Use:

      • Orthogonality index — the inner product between IMFs should be near zero. Large off-diagonal entries indicate mode mixing.
      • Reconstruction error — $\| x - \sum_k u_k - r \|$. For VMD this should be near machine epsilon; for EMD usually small but nonzero.
      • Centre-frequency separation (VMD) — the centre frequencies $\omega_k$ should be well separated; clustered $\omega_k$ means $K$ is too large.
      • Spectral overlap — pairwise correlation of mode power spectra. High overlap is mode mixing. 
      def orthogonality_index(imfs):
        """ IO ∈ [0,1]; 0 = perfectly orthogonal, larger = more mode mixing. """
        K = imfs.shape[0]
        x = imfs.sum(axis=0)
        denom = np.sum(x ** 2)
        num   = 0.0
        for j in range(K):
            for k in range(K):
                if j != k:
                    num += np.abs(np.sum(imfs[j] * imfs[k]))
        return num / denom

      The downstream-task metric

      A filter is only as good as the model trained on its output.

      For ML pipelines the only metric that ultimately matters is the one you optimise the downstream model against. If a beautifully white residual coexists with worse forecast RMSE, the filter is removing predictive information. Always compare against the identity filter baseline (no preprocessing) on the same model, the same split, the same loss. Anything that does not beat that baseline is failing.

      § 08

      Complexity, memory, and other diagnostics

      Beyond stationarity and trend, there are properties of a time series that affect what kind of model can fit it and how aggressively you can preprocess. They are worth measuring before you commit.

      Hurst exponent and long-range dependence

      The Hurst exponent $H \in (0,1)$ characterises the long-memory behaviour of a series. $H = 0.5$ is the random-walk / Brownian benchmark. $H > 0.5$ is persistent (long-range positive correlation); $H < 0.5$ is anti-persistent (mean-reverting on long timescales). Time series with $H$ far from 0.5 deserve special attention — naive ARMA models will mis-specify their long-run behaviour. 

      import nolds
H = nolds.hurst_rs(x)
print(f"Hurst exponent (R/S) : {H:.3f}")

      Detrended Fluctuation Analysis

      DFA estimates a scaling exponent $\alpha$ in a way that is robust to non-stationarity. The series is integrated, divided into windows of length $n$, locally detrended, and the root-mean-square fluctuation $F(n)$ is regressed in log–log against $n$. $\alpha \approx 0.5$ indicates uncorrelated noise; $\alpha \approx 1.0$ indicates $1/f$ noise; $\alpha \approx 1.5$ indicates Brownian motion. 

      alpha = nolds.dfa(x)
print(f"DFA scaling exponent : {alpha:.3f}")

      Sample entropy and approximate entropy

      Both quantify regularity / predictability. Lower values mean more regular (more predictable); higher values mean more random. Useful both as features for classification (e.g. healthy vs pathological physiological signals) and as diagnostics of how much structure remains after preprocessing. 

      samp_en = nolds.sampen(x, emb_dim=2)
print(f"Sample entropy       : {samp_en:.4f}")

      Mutual information — for feature selection and lag choice

      The mutual information $I(X_t; X_{t-k})$ is a nonlinear generalisation of autocorrelation. The first local minimum of $I$ as a function of lag $k$ is the classical heuristic for choosing the embedding delay in nonlinear dynamics, and it is a good lag-feature selector when relationships are nonlinear. 

      from sklearn.feature_selection import mutual_info_regression

def lagged_mi(x, max_lag=30):
    out = []
    for k in range(1, max_lag + 1):
        out.append(mutual_info_regression(x[:-k].reshape(-1, 1), x[k:])[0])
    return np.array(out)

      Causality — Granger and beyond

      When you have more than one series, the question of which one drives which becomes pressing. There is no test that establishes causality from observational data alone — that requires interventions. What we can test is whether one series helps predict another beyond what the latter's own past already explains. This is the statistical content of Granger's idea.

      Granger causality. A time series $Y$ Granger-causes $X$ if, in a regression of $X_t$ on its own past, adding the past of $Y$ produces a statistically significant reduction in residual variance. Formally, compare

      $$ X_t = \alpha + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\beta_i X_{t-i} + u_t \quad\text{(restricted)} $$ $$ X_t = \alpha + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\beta_i X_{t-i} + \sum_{j=1}^{p}\gamma_j Y_{t-j} + e_t \quad\text{(unrestricted)} $$

      and test $H_0: \gamma_1 = \dots = \gamma_p = 0$ with an F-test or likelihood ratio.

      Two cautions deserve banner-sized type. First, Granger causality is predictive, not causal in the interventional sense. $Y$ Granger-causing $X$ is consistent with $Y \to X$, but also with a common cause $Z \to (X, Y)$ where $Y$ happens to respond faster. Second, Granger causality assumes linearity and stationarity. Apply it to non-stationary data and you can get arbitrary spurious results. 

      from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import grangercausalitytests
import pandas as pd

      Vector autoregression and impulse-response analysis

      Granger causality is a hypothesis test inside a vector autoregression (VAR). A fitted VAR also gives you impulse-response functions (how a unit shock to $Y$ propagates through $X$ over time) and a variance decomposition (what fraction of forecast variance in $X$ is attributable to shocks in $Y$). These are richer diagnostics than the Granger p-value alone. 

      from statsmodels.tsa.api import VAR

model = VAR(df)
selected = model.select_order(maxlags=12)
print(selected.summary())            # AIC/BIC/HQIC across lag choices

res = model.fit(maxlags=selected.aic, ic="aic")
res.plot_acorr()                     # residual ACFs — should look white
irf = res.irf(20)
irf.plot(orth=True)                  # orthogonalised impulse response

      Transfer Entropy — the information-theoretic cousin

      Transfer entropy (Schreiber, 2000) generalises Granger causality to nonlinear and non-Gaussian settings. It measures the reduction in uncertainty about $X_t$ given the past of $Y$, beyond what is already explained by the past of $X$:

      $$ T_{Y \to X} \;=\; H(X_t \mid X_{t-1:t-k}) \;-\; H(X_t \mid X_{t-1:t-k}, Y_{t-1:t-l}) $$

      It is symmetric in form but not in value — $T_{Y \to X} \neq T_{X \to Y}$ in general, which is exactly the asymmetry we want for a directional measure. Estimation is delicate; kernel and $k$-NN estimators (Kraskov–Stögbauer–Grassberger) are standard. 

      from copent import transent

te_y_to_x = transent(y_series, x_series, lag=1)
te_x_to_y = transent(x_series, y_series, lag=1)
print(f"TE  Y → X : {te_y_to_x:.4f}")
print(f"TE  X → Y : {te_x_to_y:.4f}")

      Convergent Cross Mapping (CCM)

      For deterministic nonlinear systems, Granger and Transfer Entropy can fail in informative ways. Sugihara et al. (2012) proposed CCM, based on Takens' embedding theorem: if $X$ causes $Y$, then the dynamics of $X$ can be recovered from a delay embedding of $Y$ (and the recovery improves with library length — the "convergence"). This is the right tool for ecological and physiological systems with strong nonlinearity and weak stochasticity. 

      from skccm import CCM, Embed

embed_x = Embed(x_series).embed_vectors_1d(lag=2, embed=3)
embed_y = Embed(y_series).embed_vectors_1d(lag=2, embed=3)
ccm = CCM(weights="exponential_paper", verbose=False)
sc_x_to_y, sc_y_to_x = ccm.fit_predict(embed_x, embed_y, lib_lengths=range(20, 400, 20))

      None of these methods will save you from the most common failure mode in practice, which is to feed non-stationary series into a stationary tool. Difference, detrend, or otherwise stabilise both series before testing any kind of causality — or use the cointegration framework explicitly. A Granger test on two trending series will almost always reject, and the rejection means nothing.

      § 10

      Putting it all together: the ML preprocessing pipeline

      None of the methods above belong in isolation. A real pipeline interleaves them, and the order matters. Below is a recipe that is conservative, scrutable, and that we have found generalises across electrical-load, wind, and reservoir-inflow problems.

      Step 1 — Inspect

      Plot the series, plot the ACF and PACF, plot the rolling mean and variance. Look at it. Most of what you need to know is visible.

      Step 2 — Handle missingness, outliers, calendar effects

      Impute conservatively (forward-fill or interpolation for short gaps; model-based for longer ones). Apply a Hampel or MAD filter for impulse outliers. Adjust for known holidays / DST / sensor recalibrations before any statistical test.

      Step 3 — Diagnose

      Run ADF and KPSS together. Decide trend-stationary vs unit-root vs neither. Run Mann–Kendall for monotonic trend. Compute Hurst, sample entropy, and a Ljung–Box on the raw series for a baseline.

      Step 4 — Stabilise variance

      If amplitude scales with level, take a log (or Box–Cox with estimated $\lambda$). This is independent of, and prior to, dealing with the mean.

      Step 5 — Detrend / difference / decompose

      Pick exactly one strategy and commit. Detrend if trend-stationary, difference if unit-root, decompose (STL or VMD) if you want explicit components as features. Re-run ADF/KPSS to confirm.

      Step 6 — Build features causally

      Lags, rolling statistics, exogenous variables, Fourier features for seasonality — every transformation must use only information available at time $t$. Centred windows, future-leak features, and target-leaking interactions are the single most common cause of unreproducible "great" results.

      Step 7 — Scale per fold

      Standard or robust scaling — fit on the training fold only. For deep models, consider RevIN-style reversible per-instance normalisation, which solves a great deal of the distributional drift problem at the input.

      Step 8 — Evaluate

      Compare against the identity-preprocessing baseline, on the same split, the same model, the same loss. The preprocessing pipeline is part of the model; ablate it.

      import numpy as np, pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller, kpss
from statsmodels.tsa.seasonal import STL
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

def stationarity_summary(s, regression="ct"):
    adf_p  = adfuller(s, autolag="AIC", regression=regression)[1]
    kpss_p = kpss(s, regression=regression, nlags="auto")[1]
    return {"adf_p": adf_p, "kpss_p": kpss_p,
            "stationary": adf_p < 0.05 and kpss_p > 0.05}

def preprocess_pipeline(series, period=24, train_idx=None):
    s = series.copy()

    # 1. impute short gaps, robustly cap outliers
    s = s.interpolate(limit=3).ffill().bfill()
    q1, q3 = s.quantile([0.005, 0.995])
    s = s.clip(q1, q3)

    # 2. diagnose
    diag = stationarity_summary(s)

    # 3. stabilise variance (only if positive series)
    if (s > 0).all() and s.std() / s.mean() > 0.5:
        s = np.log(s)

    # 4. decompose with STL; keep residual as the "stationary" target
    stl = STL(s, period=period, robust=True).fit()
    components = pd.DataFrame({
        "trend": stl.trend,
        "seasonal": stl.seasonal,
        "resid": stl.resid,
    })

    # 5. scale on TRAIN only
    if train_idx is not None:
        scaler = StandardScaler().fit(components.loc[train_idx])
    else:
        scaler = StandardScaler().fit(components)
    scaled = pd.DataFrame(scaler.transform(components),
                          index=components.index,
                          columns=components.columns)

    return {"diag": diag, "components": components,
            "scaled": scaled, "scaler": scaler}
      Preprocess as little as you can justify. Document everything you did. Be able to reverse it.

      The deepest forecasting models today — N-BEATS, PatchTST, TimesNet, Chronos — succeed in spite of, not because of, sloppy preprocessing. Their internal layer normalisations and learned decompositions absorb a great deal of what classical pipelines did by hand. But "absorbs" is not "removes the need to think about". The cleaner the inputs, the smaller the model can be, the faster it trains, and the more interpretable its mistakes become. That is the whole argument for caring about everything in this document.
