01 Introduction

What's a PDE? (and why they're hard)

A partial differential equation describes how a field evolves in space and time.

  • Heat equation: \(\partial_t u = \alpha \nabla^2 u\) — temperature diffusion
  • Wave equation: \(\partial_{tt} u = c^2 \nabla^2 u\) — vibrations, light, sound
  • Navier-Stokes: \(\partial_t \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u}\) — fluid flow
  • Schrodinger: \(i\hbar \partial_t \psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \psi + V\psi\) — quantum mechanics

They're the language of physics. And solving them exactly is possible only for the simplest cases.

The challenge: we need the solution \(u(x,t)\) on a continuous domain, but we can only compute on discrete computers.
02 Classical Numerics

Classical numerics — the standard toolkit

Three families, all follow the same pattern:

  1. Discretize the domain into a mesh / grid
  2. Approximate derivatives with finite differences, integrals, or weak forms
  3. Solve the resulting (large) algebraic system
Method How it works Best for
FDM Derivatives → finite difference stencils Simple geometries, regular grids
FEM Weak formulation + basis functions Complex geometries, structured meshes
FVM Integral conservation on control volumes Conservation laws, CFD

They all share the same bottleneck: you need a mesh first.

03 The Limitation

The mesh bottleneck

Mesh generation costs scale badly in several dimensions:

1DSimple
2DModerate
3D+Hard

Quality degrades with complex geometries, shocks, boundary layers

High-dimensional problems (\(d \ge 5\)) suffer the curse of dimensionality — mesh points explode exponentially.

A 1D mesh with 100 points. A 3D mesh with 100 points per dimension = 1 million points. Per 10D = \(10^{20}\) points — impossible.
04 The Idea

The PINN idea — a different approach

Instead of discretizing the domain, discretize the solution.

Replace \(u(x,t)\) with a neural network \(u_\theta(x,t)\):

\[\underbrace{u(x,t) \;\approx\; u_\theta(x,t;\theta)}_{\text{neural network approximates the field}}\]

The network takes spatial and temporal coordinates as inputs and outputs the field value.

Key insight: We don't need a mesh to differentiate the network. We just call autodiff(u_θ, x).

Notes
05 Universal Approximation

Why neural networks?

The universal approximation theorem (Cybenko, 1989):

A feedforward network with at least one hidden layer and a smooth activation function can approximate any continuous function on a compact subset of \(\mathbb{R}^d\) to arbitrary accuracy.

This means:

\[\forall \varepsilon > 0,\;\exists\; \theta \text{ such that } \sup_{x\in\Omega} |u(x) - u_\theta(x;\theta)| < \varepsilon\]

The network is a flexible function ansatz — no assumptions about the shape of the solution.

But approximation capacity alone doesn't solve a PDE. We need to constrain the network with physics.

06 The Enabler

Automatic differentiation — the magic

Traditional numerics approximate derivatives numerically:

\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \;\approx\; \frac{u(x+h) - u(x)}{h} \quad\text{(finite difference, $O(h)$ error)}\]

Autodiff computes derivatives exactly (up to machine precision):

\[\frac{\partial u_\theta}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial u_\theta}{\partial h_1} \cdot \frac{\partial h_1}{\partial x} \quad\text{(chain rule, applied by the framework)}\]

Because \(u_\theta\) is a composition of differentiable functions (matrix mult, activation), every derivative is just another forward or backward pass through the network.

  • \(\partial_x u_\theta\) — forward-mode autodiff (Jacobian-vector product)
  • \(\partial_{xx} u_\theta\) — chain through again (Hessian-vector products)
  • No stencil, no \(h\), no truncation error.
This is why smooth activations matter. tanh, silu, sin are infinitely differentiable. ReLU is not — second derivatives don't exist at kinks.
Notes
07 Building the Residual

Building the residual — step by step

Given a PDE \(\mathcal{N}[u](x,t) = 0\), the residual is what you get when you plug the network into the PDE:

Step 1: Forward pass — get the solution estimate

\[\hat{u} = u_\theta(x, t)\]

Step 2: Autodiff — compute the derivatives the PDE needs

\[\hat{u}_t = \frac{\partial u_\theta}{\partial t}, \quad \hat{u}_x = \frac{\partial u_\theta}{\partial x}, \quad \hat{u}_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 u_\theta}{\partial x^2}\]

Step 3: Plug into the operator

\[r_\theta(x,t) = \mathcal{N}[\hat{u}] = \hat{u}_t + \hat{u}\,\hat{u}_x - \nu\,\hat{u}_{xx}\]

If the network is a good approximation, \(r_\theta \approx 0\) everywhere. The residual measures how wrong it is.

Notes
08 Loss Function

The loss function — physics as soft constraints

Training minimizes a weighted composite loss:

\[\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \lambda_r\,\mathcal{L}_r + \lambda_b\,\mathcal{L}_b + \lambda_0\,\mathcal{L}_0 + \lambda_d\,\mathcal{L}_d\]
Term Formula Enforces
Physics \(\mathcal{L}_r\) \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{N_r}\sum_i \|r_\theta(x_r^i,t_r^i)\|^2\) PDE satisfied at collocation points
Boundary \(\mathcal{L}_b\) \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{N_b}\sum_i \|\mathcal{B}[u_\theta](x_b^i,t_b^i)\|^2\) Boundary conditions
Initial \(\mathcal{L}_0\) \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{N_0}\sum_i \|u_\theta(x_0^i,0) - u_0(x_0^i)\|^2\) Initial conditions
Data \(\mathcal{L}_d\) \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{N_d}\sum_j \|u_\theta(x_d^j,t_d^j) - u_{\text{obs}}^j\|^2\) Observational data (optional)

Each term is an MSE over sampled points — different points for each term.

Notes
09 The Training Loop

The full training loop — the concept

One big computational graph. Five steps per iteration:

  1. Sample collocation points \((x, t)\) randomly
  2. Forward pass — evaluate the network to get \(\hat{u}\)
  3. Autodiff — compute \(\hat{u}_t, \hat{u}_x, \hat{u}_{xx}\) via chain rule
  4. Residual — plug into the PDE operator: \(r_\theta = \mathcal{N}[\hat{u}]\)
  5. Optimize — composite loss → backward pass → update

Every term is differentiable. Physics, BCs, ICs, data — all in one graph.

Notes
09 The Training Loop

The full training loop — the code

for each training step:
    # Sample collocation points
    (x_r, t_r) = sample(N_r)

    # Forward pass
    u = network(x_r, t_r)              # shape: (N_r, 1)

    # Autodiff derivatives
    u_t = grad(sum(u), t_r).sum()       # ∂u_θ/∂t
    u_x = grad(sum(u), x_r).sum()       # ∂u_θ/∂x
    u_xx = grad(sum(u_x), x_r).sum()    # ∂²u_θ/∂x²

    # PDE residual
    r = u_t + u * u_x - nu * u_xx      # Burgers residual
    L_r = mean(r ** 2)

    # Sample boundary/initial/data points
    (x_b, t_b) = sample_boundary(N_b)
    (x_0, )     = sample_initial(N_0)
    L_b = mean((network(x_b, t_b) - u_BC) ** 2)
    L_0 = mean((network(x_0, 0) - u_IC) ** 2)

    # Composite loss
    L = λ_r * L_r + λ_b * L_b + λ_0 * L_0

    # Backward pass
    L.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()

10 Architecture

Architecture choices that matter — depth, width, normalization

Depth / width:

  • 8–10 layers, 100–256 neurons is a common starting point
  • Deeper ≠ always better — overfitting to collocation noise
  • Positional encoding (Fourier features) helps high-frequency solutions

Input normalization:

\[\tilde{x} = 2\frac{x - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}} - 1 \qquad\text{(map to [-1, 1])}\]
Rule of thumb: normalize all inputs to roughly the same scale. A PDE with \(x \in [0, 10^6]\) and \(t \in [0, 1]\) will train terribly without normalization.
11 PIKANs

PIKANs — Kolmogorov-Arnold networks for physics

Reference: Liu, J. et al. (2024), KAN: Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks — and subsequent physics-informed variants

Standard MLPs: fixed activation functions on edges, learnable weights at nodes:

\[f(x) = \sum_k w_k \cdot \sigma(W_k x + b_k)\]

KANs flip this: learnable activation functions on edges, static weights at nodes:

\[f(x) = \sum_k \phi_k(x_k)\]

where \(\phi_k\) are learnable spline functions (not fixed sigmoid/tanh). The network learns the functions themselves, not just scalar weights.

Notes
11 PIKANs

PIKANs — why it matters for PINNs

Why KANs for PINNs:

  • Fewer parameters for same accuracy — 10-100× fewer than MLPs
  • Learnable activations adapt to solution structure — no guess tanh vs sin vs silu
  • Interpretability — learned activations reveal the underlying functional form
  • Higher-order derivatives are more accurate — splines are \(C^\infty\), autodiff through splines is exact

Trade-offs:

  • Slower per step (spline eval > scalar mult)
  • Less mature tooling — MLPs have everything pre-built
  • Heavier memory per parameter (spline coefficients vs single weight)
For smooth solutions, a shallow KAN can outperform a deep MLP in accuracy with a fraction of parameters.
12 Burgers Equation

Concrete example — Burgers' equation

A minimal nonlinear PDE that captures real physics:

\[\partial_t u + u\,\partial_x u = \nu\,\partial_{xx} u, \quad x\in[-1,1],\; t\in[0,1]\]
  • Viscosity: \(\nu = 0.01/\pi\) (small → sharp shocks form)
  • Initial condition: \(u(x,0) = -\sin(\pi x)\)
  • Boundary condition: \(u(-1,t) = u(1,t) = 0\)

Why Burgers? It's the simplest PDE with both:

  • A nonlinear convective term (\(u\,u_x\))
  • A diffusive term (\(\nu\,u_{xx}\))
  • Shock formation when \(\nu\) is small

The exact solution develops a shock layer near \(x \approx 0.5\) — a steep gradient that challenges every numerics method.

Notes
13 Burgers Residual

Burgers' residual — the math operator

The PDE operator applied to the network:

\[r_\theta = \underbrace{\partial_t u_\theta}_{\text{time derivative}} \;\;+\;\; \underbrace{u_\theta \cdot \partial_x u_\theta}_{\text{nonlinear convection}} \;\;-\;\; \underbrace{\nu \cdot \partial_{xx} u_\theta}_{\text{viscous diffusion}}\]

Three pieces:

  1. Time derivative — \(\partial_t u_\theta\) via autodiff
  2. Nonlinear convection — \(u_\theta \cdot \partial_x u_\theta\) (this is what makes Burgers hard)
  3. Viscous diffusion — \(\nu \cdot \partial_{xx} u_\theta\) (second derivative, needs smooth activations)

If the network is good, \(r_\theta \approx 0\) everywhere. The residual measures how wrong it is.

13 Burgers Residual

Burgers' residual — from math to PyTorch

In PyTorch, this is four lines:

# u_θ(x, t) — shape (N, 1)
u = net(torch.cat([x, t], dim=1))

# Derivatives via torch.autograd
u_t = torch.autograd.grad(u, t, grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(u),
                          create_graph=True, only_inputs=True)[0]
u_x = torch.autograd.grad(u, x, grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(u),
                          create_graph=True, only_inputs=True)[0]
u_xx = torch.autograd.grad(u_x, x, grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(u_x),
                           create_graph=True, only_inputs=True)[0]

# Residual
r = u_t + u * u_x - nu * u_xx

That's it. The autodiff infrastructure handles every derivative, no matter how deep.

14 Burgers Training

Training Burgers — what to expect

Typical training setup:

Hyperparameter Value
Network 8 layers × 100 neurons, tanh
Collocation points \(N_r = 10{,}000\) (Latin hypercube)
BC / IC points \(N_b = 500\), \(N_0 = 500\)
Learning rate \(10^{-3}\) (Adam), then \(10^{-4}\)
Epochs 50 000 – 100 000

What goes right:

  • After \(\sim 10^4\) steps, the solution looks roughly correct
  • The shock is visible but smeared

What goes wrong:

  • The shock region needs many more points → residual hotspot
  • Low learning rate helps but training is slow
  • Final error in the shock: 5–10% of the peak gradient
Key insight: Burgers with small \(\nu\) is a hard test case. Easy PDEs (smooth solutions, large \(\nu\)) converge in a few thousand steps. The hard cases reveal the real limitations.
15 Heat Equation

Example — 1D heat equation

\[\partial_t u = \alpha \, \partial_{xx} u, \quad x \in [0,1], \; t \in [0,0.1]\]
  • Diffusivity: \(\alpha = 0.01\)
  • Initial condition: \(u(x,0) = \sin(\pi x)\)
  • Boundary conditions: \(u(0,t) = u(1,t) = 0\)

Exact solution (for verification):

\[u(x,t) = e^{-\alpha \pi^2 t} \sin(\pi x)\]

This is the simplest non-trivial PDE with time dependence. The solution smoothly decays — no shocks, no nonlinearities — making it a clean test of PINN basics.

15 Heat Equation

Heat equation — PyTorch implementation

import torch

# Network
net = torch.nn.Sequential(
    torch.nn.Linear(2, 128), torch.nn.Tanh(),
    torch.nn.Linear(128, 128), torch.nn.Tanh(),
    torch.nn.Linear(128, 1),
)

# Collocation points (training)
x_col = torch.rand(2000, 1) * 1
y_col = torch.rand(2000, 1) * 0.1
x_col.requires_grad_(True)
y_col.requires_grad_(True)

# Residual: u_t - alpha * u_xx = 0
v = net(torch.cat([x_col, y_col], dim=1))
u_t = torch.autograd.grad(v.sum(), y_col,
    grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(v),
    create_graph=True, only_inputs=True)[0]
u_xx = torch.autograd.grad(v.sum(), x_col,
    grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(v),
    create_graph=True, only_inputs=True)[0]
u_xx = torch.autograd.grad(u_xx.sum(), x_col,
    grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(u_xx),
    create_graph=True, only_inputs=True)[0]
r = u_t - alpha * u_xx

# BC + IC residuals
v_bc = net(torch.cat([torch.zeros(100,1), y_col[:100]], dim=1))
v_ic = net(torch.cat([x_col[:100], torch.zeros(100,1)], dim=1))
L = r.pow(2).mean() + v_bc.pow(2).mean() + (v_ic - u_exact(x_col[:100])).pow(2).mean()
L.backward()
opt.step()
16 Circuit Example

Inverse example — circuit voltage and current

Telegrapher's equation for a lossy transmission line:

\[\partial_{xx} V(x,t) = LC \, \partial_{tt} V + (RC + LG) \, \partial_t V + RG \, V\]

Same equation for current \(I(x,t)\) — just different boundary conditions.

  • \(L\) = inductance per unit length, \(C\) = capacitance per unit length
  • \(R\) = resistance per unit length, \(G\) = conductance per unit length

Why this is interesting for PINNs:

  • Second-order in both space and time — two time derivatives
  • Damped wave equation — underdamped (oscillatory), critically damped, or overdamped regimes
  • Engineers measure voltage and current at ports — not the full field
  • Perfect for inverse problems: discover \(R, L, C, G\) from port measurements
Notes

The same residual pattern works for voltage and current — just swap the PDE coefficients. Inverse problem: make \(R, L, C, G\) trainable and fit to oscilloscope measurements at the ports.
17 Inverse Problems

The killer app — inverse problems

In classical numerics, forward and inverse problems are totally different:

  • Forward: solve the PDE (one algorithm)
  • Inverse: design a whole separate optimization loop

In PINNs, they're the same code — just flip what's trained.

Forward: known \(\nu\), find \(u\)

\[\min_\theta \mathcal{L}*r(u*\theta; \nu) + \mathcal{L}_b + \mathcal{L}_0\]

Inverse: unknown \(\nu\), find \(u\) and \(\nu\)

\[\min_{\theta,\,\nu} \mathcal{L}*r(u*\theta; \nu) + \mathcal{L}_b + \mathcal{L}_0 + \lambda_d\,\mathcal{L}_d\]

The parameter \(\nu\) becomes a trainable scalar in the residual computation. Same loop, one extra variable.

This is genuinely hard for classical methods. You'd need a nested PDE solver inside an outer optimizer. PINNs do it natively — the autodiff graph includes the physics parameters.

18 Inverse Example

Inverse example — discovering the diffusion coefficient

Scenario: You have sparse, noisy measurements of a temperature field and want to discover the diffusivity \(\alpha\).

Setup:

  • Measurements: \(100\) points scattered in space-time, noise \(\sigma = 0.05\)
  • True \(\alpha = 0.001\)
  • Network: \(6 \times 128\), tanh
  • \(\alpha\) is a trainable parameter (not fixed)

Loss:

\[\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_i (\partial_t u_\theta - \alpha\,\nabla^2 u_\theta)*i^2 + \frac{\lambda_d}{N_d}\sum_j (u*\theta(x_j) - u_j^{\text{obs}})^2\]

Result: The optimizer converges to \(\hat{\alpha} = 0.00102 \pm 0.00008\) — within measurement noise.

The physics term regularizes the parameter estimation. Without it, 100 noisy measurements can't identify \(\alpha\). The PDE is the structural prior that makes inference possible.
19 Spectral Bias

The spectral bias problem

Neural networks have a well-known bias: they learn low-frequency functions first.

Empirical observation (Raissi et al., 2020):

80%Low frequencies fitted
20%High frequencies lag

Why this matters for PINNs:

  • Shock layers, boundary layers, and oscillatory solutions are high-frequency
  • The network fits the smooth part first, takes thousands of steps to capture sharp features
  • The residual at the shock stays large → training stalls

Visual: imagine the network approximating a square wave. It fits the flat parts instantly, then slowly "squeezes" the transition. The Gibbs-like ringing appears late.

Notes
20 Fixing Spectral Bias

Fixing spectral bias — Fourier features

Method 1: Fourier features (Mesnard et al., 2022)

Pre-process inputs with random Fourier features before the network:

\[\gamma(x) = [\sin(2\pi Bx),\;\cos(2\pi Bx)]\]

where \(B\) is a random projection matrix (scaled by a frequency parameter \(\sigma\)).

The network now operates on high-frequency inputs and learns a smooth mapping — effectively shifting the spectrum.

Notes
20 Fixing Spectral Bias

Fixing spectral bias — SIREN activations

Method 2: SIREN activations (Sitzmann et al., 2020)

Use sin activations instead of tanh:

\[\text{SIREN}(x) = \sin(Wx)\]

The derivative of sin is cos — same frequency band, no decay. tanh derivatives decay to zero for large inputs, killing gradient flow for high frequencies.

Result on Burgers' equation: SIREN + Fourier features can resolve shock layers 10× sharper than vanilla tanh.

21 Loss Balancing

Loss balancing — the multi-term gradient problem

The composite loss has terms with wildly different scales:

\[\mathcal{L} = \lambda_r\,\mathcal{L}_r + \lambda_b\,\mathcal{L}_b + \lambda_0\,\mathcal{L}_0\]

Typical magnitudes during training:

Term Typical range
\(\mathcal{L}_r\) \(10^{-1}\) – \(10^2\)
\(\mathcal{L}_b\) \(10^{-3}\) – \(10^0\)
\(\mathcal{L}_0\) \(10^{-2}\) – \(10^1\)

The problem: if \(\mathcal{L}_r \gg \mathcal{L}_b\), the gradient is dominated by the physics term. Boundary conditions are violated because the optimizer doesn't "see" them.

What NOT to do: set \(\lambda_r = \lambda_b = \lambda_0 = 1\) and hope. This rarely works.

Notes
22 Loss Strategies

Loss balancing — heuristic and gradient strategies

Strategy 1: Heuristic scaling (most common in practice)

Normalize each term by its initial value:

\[\lambda_r(t) = \frac{\mathcal{L}_r(0)}{\mathcal{L}_r(t)},\quad \lambda_b(t) = \frac{\mathcal{L}_b(0)}{\mathcal{L}_b(t)}\]

Strategy 2: Gradient balancing (Wang et al., 2021)

Match the gradient norms of each term:

\[\|\nabla_\theta (\lambda_r \mathcal{L}*r)\| = \|\nabla*\theta (\lambda_b \mathcal{L}_b)\|\]

Solve for \(\lambda_r, \lambda_b\) each step.

Notes
22 Loss Strategies

Loss balancing — NTK weights and practical advice

Strategy 3: NTK-based weights (Shukla & Diost, 2021)

Use the neural tangent kernel to estimate each term's learning speed and weight accordingly. More principled, but more expensive.

Practical rule: start with heuristic scaling. If training is unstable, switch to gradient balancing. NTK methods are overkill for most applications.
23 Optimizers

PINN optimizers — why Adam is not enough

Standard deep learning uses Adam. PINN training is fundamentally different:

  • Multi-objective loss: physics + boundary + initial + data terms with competing gradients
  • Non-stationary: loss landscape shifts dramatically as training progresses
  • No validation set: collocation points are the only signal — no test loss to monitor

The standard two-stage approach:

  1. Adam (with LR warmup + cosine annealing) for \(\sim 50{,}000\) steps — fast initial descent
  2. L-BFGS for \(\sim 5{,}000\) steps — quasi-Newton refinement near the minimum

Why two-stage:

  • Adam handles the noisy, multi-term gradient well initially
  • L-BFGS converges to higher accuracy — critical for residual \(\approx 0\) to machine precision
  • L-BFGS on its own from initialization fails (Hessian estimate too noisy)

Easy win, widely reproducible.

Notes
23 Optimizers

PINN optimizers — advanced methods

The problem Adam/L-BFGS misses: the composite loss has conflicting gradients — the Hessian is ill-conditioned because physics, boundary, and data terms pull in different directions.

SOAP (Self-Scaled Quasi-Newton, Kiyani et al. 2019):

  • Self-scaling factor adapts per-dimension, no manual Hessian pre-conditioning
  • Directly tackles multi-objective gradient conflicts
  • Empirically outperforms L-BFGS on PINN loss landscapes

D-NGD (Deep Natural Gradient Descent, Sholokhov et al. 2022):

  • Uses the Fisher information matrix as the metric — accounts for the function-space geometry, not parameter-space
  • Naturally preconditions the conflicting gradients in the composite loss
  • Best-possible error rates, but expensive (Fisher-vector products scale with model size)

When to use which:

Method Cost Best for
Adam + L-BFGS Low Baseline, widely reproducible
SOAP / Self-Scaled quasi-Newton Medium High recommendation — better preconditioning
D-NGD High Research-grade, largest models, best possible error
These methods shine because they better precondition the conflicting gradients that appear in the composite PINN loss. The physics term dominates early, boundary terms dominate late — a good optimizer adapts to this shift.
24 gPINNs

Gradient-enhanced PINNs (gPINNs) — the problem

Reference: Yu, J., Lu, L., Meng, X., & Karniadakis, G. E. (2022), CMAME — DOI: 10.1016/j.cma.2022.114823

The residual loss only penalizes \(r_\theta \approx 0\) at collocation points. But in regions with steep gradients, the residual can be near-zero without the solution being accurate.

The fix: also penalize the gradient of the residual w.r.t. inputs:

\[\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_i |r(\mathbf{x}*i;\theta)|^2 + \lambda \,\frac{1}{N_r}\sum_i |\nabla*{\mathbf{x}} r(\mathbf{x}_i;\theta)|^2\]

This "second-gradient" regularization enforces both \(r \approx 0\) and \(\nabla r \approx 0\) at collocation points.

Notes
24 gPINNs

Gradient-enhanced PINNs (gPINNs) — why it works

Why gPINNs work:

  • \(\nabla_\mathbf{x} r = \nabla_\mathbf{x} \mathcal{N}[u_\theta]\) involves third-order derivatives of \(u_\theta\) — higher than the original PDE order
  • Penalizing it forces the solution to be smooth in its residual, not just small
  • Especially effective with steep gradients or fewer collocation points
  • Helps inverse problems — extra gradient signal constrains parameter estimation

Cost: one extra autodiff pass. Minimal overhead.

gPINNs are the simplest high-accuracy upgrade to vanilla PINNs — just add a gradient term to the loss. Often more effective than spectral-bias fixes for shock-dominated problems.
25 RAR

Residual-Based Adaptive Refinement (RAR)

Reference: Yu, J., Lu, L., Jin, L., & Karniadakis, G. E. (2020), CMAME — Residual-based adaptive refinement for PINNs

Collocation points are static by default. But shocks live in tiny regions — wasting points on smooth areas.

RAR redistributes points during tra-ining:

  1. Train for \(N\) steps on current points
  2. Evaluate residual \(|r_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i)|\) at all collocation points
  3. Remove points in low-residual regions
  4. Generate new points in high-residual regions (e.g. Latin hypercube sampling weighted by \(|r_\theta|\))
  5. Continue training with the updated point set
  6. Repeat every \(K\) steps

Why it works:

  • Points concentrate where the solution is hard — shocks, boundary layers, steep gradients
  • Same total \(N_r\) but far more efficient allocation
  • Often 2-5× faster convergence than uniform sampling
  • No extra hyperparameters beyond the redistribution interval \(K\)

Cost: one extra residual evaluation pass + sampling step. Negligible.

RAR + gPINNs together are the most impactful low-effort upgrades to vanilla PINNs for shock-dominated problems. Both cost almost nothing and give measurable gains.
Notes
26 Causality

Causality violation in time-dependent PINNs

PINNs are global in time — the network sees all \(t \in [0,T]\) in every batch.

This can cause a subtle failure:

The network fits late times well (where the residual is smaller) and ignores early times (where the initial condition and transients are harder).

The PDE solution is causal — early times determine late times. But the PINN doesn't know this.

Mitigation:

  1. Causal training (Agarwal et al., 2022): sample \(t\) in increasing order, use a masking loss
  2. Time-marching: solve in sub-domains \([0,T_1], [T_1,T_2], \ldots\)
  3. Weight early times more heavily in the loss
For steady-state problems (\(\partial_t = 0\)), this issue doesn't arise. It's specific to time-dependent PDEs.

Variant Key idea When to use

27 Variants

Domain decomposition — XPINNs

Reference: Jagtap et al. — Extended PINNs

Split \(\Omega\) into \(k\) non-overlapping subdomains \(\Omega_1, \ldots, \Omega_k\). Each subdomain gets its own network \(u_{\theta_i}\):

\[\mathcal{L} = \sum_{i=1}^k \bigg( \mathcal{L}*r(\Omega_i) + \mathcal{L}*b(\partial\Omega_i \cap \partial\Omega) + \sum*{\text{interfaces } \Gamma*{ij}} \gamma_{ij} \|u_{\theta_i} - u_{\theta_j}\|*{\Gamma*{ij}}^2 \bigg)\]

Key features:

  • Each network trained independently → embarrassingly parallel
  • Interface continuity enforced via penalty terms
  • No global collocation points needed — each network only sees its own subdomain

When it shines:

  • Large domains where one network can't capture the solution
  • Parallel compute available (GPU clusters, HPC)
  • Subdomains can have different physics (different PDE operators)
27 Variants

Domain decomposition — cPINNs (conservation) cPINNs

Reference: Jagtap, Kaus & Karniadakis

XPINNs enforce pointwise continuity at interfaces. cPINNs enforce weak conservation:

\[\int_{\Gamma_{ij}} \big[ \mathbf{f}(u_{\theta_i}) - \mathbf{f}(u_{\theta_j}) \big] \cdot \mathbf{n} \; dS = 0\]

Instead of \(u_i = u_j\) pointwise, the flux is conserved across interfaces — a weaker, physically motivated constraint.

When cPINNs > XPINNs:

  • Conservation laws — shocks, discontinuities, Riemann problems
  • The flux-conservation constraint respects the integral form of the PDE
  • Pointwise continuity fails at discontinuities; flux conservation does not

When XPINNs > cPINNs:

  • Smooth solutions — pointwise continuity is tighter and simpler
  • Different PDEs on different subdomains (XPINNs allow different operators)
Domain decomposition is where PINNs converge with classical FEM. The networks are basis functions; the interfaces are the mesh boundaries. Same mathematics, different parameterization.
28 Operator Learning

Beyond PINNs — operator learning

PINNs learn one solution for one problem instance.

Operator learning asks: can we learn the solution operator itself?

\[\mathcal{G}: \text{IC/BC/coefficients} \;\mapsto\; \text{solution } u(x,t)\]
Method What it learns Inference cost
PINN One solution, one PDE instance Slow (train per instance)
DeepONet Solution operator from data Fast (evaluate network)
Fourier Neural Operator Solution operator via Fourier layers Fast

Physics-informed DeepONets: Combine both — the operator is constrained by the PDE residual.

Trade-off: PINNs = physics-accurate but per-instance cost. Operators = fast but need training data. Hybrids exist and are actively researched.
Notes
29 Neural Laplace

Neural Laplace — frequency-domain PINNs

Reference: Kharazmi, E., Shi, Z. & Karniadakis, G. E. (2021), Neural Laplace

Standard PINNs sample \(t\) globally — all time steps in every batch. Neural Laplace transforms to the Laplace domain:

\[\mathcal{L}\{u(t)\}(s) = \int_0^\infty u(t) e^{-st} \, dt\]

In the Laplace domain, time derivatives become algebraic multiplications:

\[\mathcal{L}\{\partial_t u\}(s) = s \, \hat{u}(s) - u(0)\]

The PDE becomes an elliptic PDE in space + complex parameter \(s\) — solved once per \(s\) value, then inverse-transformed.

Key advantages:

  • No time-stepping — solve for all \(t\) at once via inverse Laplace transform
  • Exponential convergence — analytic structure captured exactly
  • Natural initial conditions — baked into the transform
  • Efficient for stiff problems — no stability constraints on \(\Delta t\)

Cost: inverse Laplace requires quadrature over \(\sim 10-20\) complex \(s\) values.

Neural Laplace is the most elegant way to handle time-dependent PDEs. No more time-stepping, no more CFL constraints — just solve an elliptic PDE for complex parameters.
Notes
30 Multi-fidelity

Multi-fidelity PINNs (MF-PINNs)

Reference: Wang, S., Wang, H. & Perdikaris, P. (2021), J. Comput. Phys.

Real-world data comes at different fidelities: coarse CFD simulations + sparse high-fidelity experiments. MF-PINNs combine them:

\[\mathcal{L} = \lambda_1 \mathcal{L}*{r,1} + \lambda_2 \mathcal{L}*{r,2} + \lambda_c \mathcal{L}_{\text{correlation}}\]
  • Fidelity 1 (coarse): cheap simulation, many collocation points, loose constraints
  • Fidelity 2 (fine): expensive measurements, few points, tight constraints
  • Correlation term: enforces that the difference \(u_2 - u_1\) is smooth and simple

How it works:

  1. Train two coupled networks: \(u_{\theta_1}\) (coarse) + \(u_{\theta_2}\) (fine)
  2. \(u_{\theta_1}\) sees many collocation points (cheap residual)
  3. \(u_{\theta_2}\) sees few data points but is regularized by \(u_{\theta_1}\)
  4. The correlation loss forces \(u_{\theta_2} - u_{\theta_1}\) to be a simple function

When it shines:

  • You have expensive measurements + cheap approximations
  • High-fidelity data is sparse but low-fidelity data is dense
  • Computational budget is limited
MF-PINNs let you get 80% of the accuracy at 20% of the cost by leveraging cheap coarse models to guide expensive fine models.
Notes
31 Symmetry PINNs

Symmetry-preserving PINNs

Reference: Raissi, M. (2020), Hidden physics models — and subsequent equivariant PINN work

Standard PINNs break symmetries that the PDE has. A translation-invariant PDE should give a translation-invariant solution — but the neural network doesn't know this.

Equivariant architectures enforce known symmetries:

  • Translation invariance → weight sharing (convolutional layers)
  • Rotation invariance → SO(2)/SO(3) equivariant layers
  • Gauge invariance → covariant derivatives (gauge theory)
  • Scaling invariance → homogeneous activation functions

How to build:

  1. Identify symmetries of the PDE (Noether's theorem)
  2. Build equivariant layers — e.g., group-equivariant CNNs
  3. Train normally — symmetry is hard-coded, not learned

Example: Navier-Stokes has Galilean invariance (physics looks the same in any inertial frame). A standard PINN won't respect this; an equivariant PINN enforces it by construction.

Cost: equivariant layers are more complex but fewer parameters — symmetry is a strong inductive bias.

Symmetry preservation isn't a loss trick — it's an architectural constraint. The network can only learn what its structure allows.
Notes
32 Fractional PINNs

Fractional PINNs (fPINNs)

Reference: Mao, Z., Ji, X., Karniadakis, G. E. (2020), Fractional PINNs

Standard derivatives are local: \(\partial_x u(x)\) depends only on \(u\) at \(x\). Fractional derivatives are non-local:

\[{}^C_0D_x^\alpha u(x) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(1-\alpha)} \int_0^x (x-t)^{-\alpha} \partial_t u(t) \, dt\]

Where they appear:

  • Anomalous diffusion — mean-square displacement \(\sim t^\alpha\), not \(t\) (\(\alpha \ne 1\))
  • Viscoelastic materials — memory effects, power-law relaxation
  • Long-range interactions — Lévy flights, peridynamics
  • Fractional Schrödinger equation — quantum systems with power-law tunneling

How to implement:

  1. Caputo derivative: approximate integral with quadrature
  2. Autodiff through the quadrature — the kernel \((x-t)^{-\alpha}\) is differentiable
  3. Residual includes fractional terms — same loss framework
\[\partial_t^\alpha u - \nu \partial_{xx} u = 0 \quad \text{(time-fractional diffusion)}\]

Cost: quadrature adds \(O(N)\) work per collocation point. Can be accelerated with FFT-based schemes.

Fractional derivatives model memory and non-locality — physical phenomena that standard PDEs can't capture. PINNs handle them naturally with quadrature-based autodiff.
Notes
33 Diffusion PINNs

Physics-guided diffusion models

Reference: 2024-2025 — physics-informed generative models at the intersection of diffusion models and PINNs

Diffusion models generate data by learning to reverse a noising process. PINNs enforce physical constraints. Combining them:

Forward process: add noise to data \(\mathbf{x}_0 \to \mathbf{x}_T\)

Reverse process: denoise \(\mathbf{x}_T \to \mathbf{x}_0\) with physics guidance:

\[\nabla_{\mathbf{x}_t} \log p(\mathbf{x}*t | \text{physics}) = \nabla*{\mathbf{x}*t} \log p*{\text{gen}}(\mathbf{x}*t) + \lambda \, \nabla*{\mathbf{x}*t} \mathcal{L}*{\text{PDE}}(\mathbf{x}_t)\]

Key ideas:

  • The denoiser network is guided by the PDE residual gradient at each step
  • Physics acts as a conditional prior during generation
  • Can handle highly noisy, sparse inverse problems that stump standard PINNs
  • Generates ensembles of solutions with built-in uncertainty

When to use:

  • Heavy noise — standard PINNs fail with >10% noise
  • Multiple solutions — diffusion models sample from the solution manifold
  • Data scarcity — physics guides generation from near-zero data
  • Uncertainty quantification — natural via sampling
Physics-guided diffusion models represent the frontier of PINN research. They combine the generative power of diffusion models with the physical fidelity of PINNs — handling noise, multimodality, and uncertainty that vanilla PINNs can't.

34 Failure Modes

Failure modes — diagnostics checklist

Before you trust a PINN solution, check these:

  1. Residual map — plot \(\|r_\theta(x,t)\|\) over the domain. Hotspots = problems.
  2. Loss curves — all four terms should converge to similar scales. One flat while others drop = imbalance.
  3. Solution sanity — does it respect maximum principles? Monotonicity? Conservation?
  4. Sensitivity to point count — double \(N_r\). If the solution changes significantly, it's under-resolved.
  5. Cross-validation — hold out data points. Overfitting to collocation points = bad generalization.
Notes
34 Failure Modes

Failure modes — common patterns

What goes wrong:

  • Underfitting — smooth but wrong → network too small
  • Overfitting — oscillates wildly → too many points/parameters, no regularization
  • Stuck loss term — balance problem or unsatisfiable constraint
  • Spectral bias — smooth part fits, sharp features lag → use Fourier features or SIREN
  • Causality violation — late times fit, early times ignored → time-marching or causal sampling
35 Practical Gotchas

Practical implementation — gotchas and heuristics

Point sampling:

  • Latin hypercube beats uniform for 1D marginals, but uniform random can be better in high dimensions
  • Importance sampling: sample more densely where \(|r_\text{coarse}|\) is large (coarse PINN first pass)
  • Adaptive refinement: add points where residual is high — cheaper than increasing network width
  • Boundary points matter more than you think — undersampling BCs causes oscillatory solutions

Loss weighting heuristics:

  • Start with equal weights → if one term dominates, reduce its weight or normalize by residual magnitude
  • Normalize each loss term by its initial value: \(\mathcal{L}'_i = \mathcal{L}_i / \mathcal{L}_i(0)\) — they start at similar scales
  • Dynamic weighting (GradNorm, PCGrad): monitor gradient magnitudes and adjust weights per-step
  • Rule of thumb: if one loss term is \(>10\times\) larger than others, it controls the gradient

Convergence diagnostics:

  • Plot each loss term separately — one flat while others drop = imbalance
  • Monitor gradient norms per parameter group — exploding or vanishing gradients signal bad initialization
  • Check spectral content of the solution — if high frequencies are missing, you have spectral bias
  • Double \(N_r\) and retrain — if the solution changes significantly, it was under-resolved
Most PINN failures are not fundamental — they're bad sampling, bad weighting, or under-training. Fix these before reaching for exotic architectures.

36 Mental Model

Mental model — what a PINN is

A PINN is a function approximator constrained by a differential equation:

\[\underbrace{u_\theta}*{\text{flexible neural network}} \;+\; \underbrace{\text{autodiff}}*{\text{exact derivatives, no mesh}} \;+\; \underbrace{\text{residual loss}}_{\text{physics as soft constraint}}\]

Three ingredients, no magic:

  1. The network provides the ansatz — a parameterized family of functions rich enough to represent the solution.
  2. Autodiff provides the derivatives — no discretization, no stencils, no truncation error.
  3. The residual loss provides the constraint — the PDE is no longer solved, it's enforced.
The PDE stops being something you discretize and solve. It becomes something you use to regularize a neural network. This shift in perspective is what makes PINNs powerful.
Notes
37 Takeaways

Key takeaways

  1. PINNs replace mesh-based discretization with neural network approximation + autodiff derivatives.
  1. The PDE residual becomes a loss term — physics is a soft constraint, not a hard discretization.
  1. Autodiff is the enabler — exact derivatives with zero extra cost beyond the forward pass.
  1. Inverse problems are native — unknown parameters are just extra trainable variables. Works for circuits, temperature fields, and more.
  1. Training is the challenge — spectral bias, loss balancing, and causality are active research areas with practical fixes (Fourier features, SIREN, RAR, gPINNs).
  1. Domain decomposition (XPINNs/cPINNs) enables parallel training and discontinuous solutions.
  1. Frequency-domain methods (Neural Laplace) and multi-fidelity approaches push the boundaries of what PINNs can do efficiently.
  1. Symmetry-preserving and fractional PINNs extend the framework to equivariant architectures and non-local physics.
  1. PINNs sit between classical numerics (mesh-based, accurate per-instance) and operator learning (data-driven, fast inference). They fill a middle ground: physics-faithful, flexible, good for small data.
  1. Physics-guided diffusion models represent the frontier — combining generative power with physical fidelity for noisy, uncertain, multi-modal problems.

39 Summary

Summary

  • PINNs encode a PDE as a loss via the residual \(r_\theta = \mathcal{N}[u_\theta]\).
  • Autodiff supplies exact derivatives — mesh-free by construction.
  • A composite loss blends physics, BC/IC, and optional data.
  • Inverse problems are solved with the same code as forward problems.
  • Training requires care: loss balancing, spectral bias mitigation, and causality-aware sampling for time-dependent problems.
  • PINNs sit between classical numerics and operator learning — physics-faithful and flexible.
