0 Prelude
The LP relaxation is loose; we tighten it.

Why cut?

A Mixed‑Integer Linear Program (MILP) is a linear program whose optimum we insist on finding among the lattice points of a polyhedron. Drop the integrality and you get the LP relaxation: easy to solve, but its optimum typically lives on a fractional vertex outside the convex hull of feasible integer points.

A cutting plane is a linear inequality $\pi^\top x \le \pi_0$ that

(i) is satisfied by every integer‑feasible point of the MILP, and
(ii) is violated by the current LP optimum $x^*$.

Adding the cut to the formulation chops away a piece of the LP polyhedron without ever excluding an integer solution. Iterating this is the cutting-plane method; embedding it inside branch-and-bound gives branch-and-cut, which is what every modern solver actually does.

Why is the LP relaxation loose? The LP relaxation $\max\{c^\top x : Ax \le b, x \ge 0\}$ is a convex program solvable in polynomial time. Adding the constraint $x \in \mathbb{Z}^n$ turns it into a combinatorial problem whose feasible region is a discrete (non-convex) set. The integrality gap is $z^*_{\text{LP}} / z^*_{\text{IP}}$ — how much the LP over-estimates the integer optimum. For easy problems (like totally unimodular systems), the gap is always 1: LP optima are automatically integer. For hard problems like bin-packing, the gap can be $\Omega(\log n)$ in the worst case. Cutting planes exist precisely to close this gap by adding constraints that are tight on the integer hull.
Geometric picture The LP polyhedron $P$ contains the integer hull $P_I = \operatorname{conv}(P \cap \mathbb{Z}^n)$ but is generally strictly larger. Each cut removes a slice of $P \setminus P_I$ without touching $P_I$. In the limit, families like Gomory's cuts converge to $P_I$ — though usually very slowly. In practice a small number of well-chosen cuts from multiple families (MIG, MIR, cover, clique) can close 90%+ of the gap in seconds.

This chapter walks through five classical families of cuts in order of generality: Gomory's pure fractional cut, the mixed-integer Gomory (MIG) cut, the mixed-integer rounding (MIR) inequality, cover cuts from the 0/1 knapsack, and lifting. Each section ends with a complete, runnable Python snippet.

Throughout, we use the canonical MILP

\[ \max\; c^\top x \quad \text{s.t.}\quad Ax \le b,\; x \ge 0,\; x_j \in \mathbb{Z}\;\forall j \in I, \]

with $I \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}$ the index set of integer variables. When $I = \{1,\dots,n\}$ we have a pure integer program; otherwise it is mixed.

I Gomory, 1958
The first cut from a simplex tableau row.

Gomory fractional cuts.

Solve the LP relaxation by the simplex method. If the optimum is integer‑feasible, you're done. Otherwise some basic integer variable takes a fractional value at the optimum — and Gomory showed how to read off a valid cut directly from the optimal tableau row.

Write the optimal tableau row for a basic integer variable $x_{B(i)}$ whose value $\bar b_i$ is fractional:

\[ x_{B(i)} + \sum_{j \in N} \bar a_{ij}\, x_j \;=\; \bar b_i, \]

where $N$ indexes the non‑basic variables. Split each coefficient into its integer and fractional parts:

\[ \bar a_{ij} = \lfloor \bar a_{ij} \rfloor + f_{ij}, \quad \bar b_i = \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor + f_0, \quad f_{ij}, f_0 \in [0,1). \]

The derivation is a four-line algebraic argument. Write it out once carefully and it will never be mysterious again.

Step 1 — substitute the fractional decompositions

Replace $\bar a_{ij} = \lfloor \bar a_{ij}\rfloor + f_{ij}$ and $\bar b_i = \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor + f_0$ in the tableau row:

\[ x_{B(i)} + \sum_{j \in N}\bigl(\lfloor \bar a_{ij}\rfloor + f_{ij}\bigr)x_j \;=\; \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor + f_0. \]

Step 2 — isolate the fractional parts

Move all integer-coefficient terms to the right, leaving only the fractional parts $f_{ij}$ on the left:

\[ \sum_{j \in N} f_{ij}\, x_j \;=\; f_0 \;+\; \underbrace{\lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor - x_{B(i)} - \sum_{j \in N}\lfloor \bar a_{ij}\rfloor\, x_j}_{=:\,K}. \]

The quantity $K$ is an integer whenever every $x_j$ and $x_{B(i)}$ is an integer — which is exactly our assumption.

Step 3 — bound the integer $K$ from below

Because $f_{ij} \in [0,1)$ and $x_j \in \mathbb{Z}_+$, the left-hand side satisfies

\[ \sum_{j \in N} f_{ij}\, x_j \;\ge\; 0. \]

Substituting: $f_0 + K \ge 0$, so $K \ge -f_0 > -1$. But $K$ is an integer, and the only integer strictly greater than $-1$ that also satisfies this is $K \ge 0$.

Step 4 — conclude the cut

Since $K \ge 0$ and $f_0 > 0$:

\[ \sum_{j \in N} f_{ij}\, x_j \;=\; f_0 + K \;\ge\; f_0. \]
Why is the LP optimum violated? At the LP optimum, all non‑basic variables are zero: $x_j = 0$ for every $j \in N$. Therefore the cut's left-hand side evaluates to $\sum_j f_{ij} \cdot 0 = 0$, but the right-hand side is $f_0 > 0$. The inequality $0 \ge f_0$ is false. Every Gomory cut is violated by construction at the current LP vertex.
The key number-theoretic insight The argument is essentially this: we have a real number $\sum f_{ij}x_j$ which equals $f_0 + K$ for some integer $K$. Because $f_0 \in (0,1)$, the quantity is not an integer (it sits strictly between consecutive integers $K$ and $K+1$). The only way it can be nonneg is if $K \ge 0$, pinning the sum to be at least $f_0$. This is the classical Chvátal–Gomory rounding argument — divide a valid inequality by a positive scalar, then round the right-hand side down to the nearest integer.
Gomory fractional cut If every variable (including slacks) is integer‑constrained and $\bar b_i \notin \mathbb{Z}$, then \[ \sum_{j \in N} f_{ij}\, x_j \;\ge\; f_0 \] is valid for the integer hull and violated by the current LP optimum (where all non‑basic $x_j = 0$, so the left side equals $0 < f_0$).

Note the violation is structural: at the LP optimum every non‑basic variable is zero, so the cut's left‑hand side evaluates to zero, but $f_0 > 0$. Every Gomory cut cuts off the current vertex by construction.

Integrality of slacks The argument needs all variables in the tableau row to be integer. If $A$ and $b$ have integer entries and the original variables are integer, slacks $s = b - Ax$ are integer too — so the pure Gomory cut applies. Otherwise we need the mixed‑integer variant (next chapter).

A worked example

Consider

\[ \max\; 5x_1 + 8x_2 \quad \text{s.t.}\;\; \begin{aligned} x_1 + x_2 &\le 6\\ 5x_1 + 9x_2 &\le 45\\ x_1, x_2 &\in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}. \end{aligned} \]

Introduce slacks $s_1, s_2$. The LP relaxation has optimum $x_1 = 9/4,\; x_2 = 15/4$ with both $s_1, s_2$ non‑basic. The tableau row for $x_1$ reads

\[ x_1 + \tfrac{9}{4} s_1 - \tfrac{1}{4} s_2 = \tfrac{9}{4}. \]

So $f_0 = 1/4$, $f_{s_1} = 1/4$, $f_{s_2} = 3/4$ (since $-1/4 - \lfloor -1/4 \rfloor = -1/4 + 1 = 3/4$). The cut is

\[ \tfrac{1}{4} s_1 + \tfrac{3}{4} s_2 \;\ge\; \tfrac{1}{4} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; s_1 + 3 s_2 \ge 1. \]

Substituting $s_1 = 6 - x_1 - x_2$ and $s_2 = 45 - 5x_1 - 9x_2$ gives the cut in the original space:

\[ 4x_1 + 7x_2 \le 35. \]

At the LP optimum: $4(9/4) + 7(15/4) = 35.25 > 35$ — cut off. Every integer feasible point satisfies it.

Worked example — generating a Gomory cut

import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import linprog

# max 5 x1 + 8 x2
# s.t. x1 + x2 <= 6
#      5 x1 + 9 x2 <= 45,  x integer >= 0
c = np.array([-5.0, -8.0])                 # linprog minimizes
A = np.array([[1.0, 1.0], [5.0, 9.0]])
b = np.array([6.0, 45.0])

res = linprog(c, A_ub=A, b_ub=b, bounds=(0, None), method="highs")
x_lp = res.x                                # [2.25, 3.75]

# --- reconstruct the optimal basis manually ---
# Standard form: A x + I s = b, both x1, x2 are basic.
B  = A                                      # basis columns from A
Bi = np.linalg.inv(B)
# Slack columns in [A | I] for s1, s2 are the 2x2 identity; B^{-1} I = B^{-1}
abar_slacks = Bi                            # row i, column j gives the
                                            # tableau entry for x_{B(i)} on s_j
bbar = Bi @ b                               # [2.25, 3.75]

# Pick the first fractional basic variable (row 0 -> x1 = 9/4)
i = 0
row = abar_slacks[i]                        # [9/4, -1/4]
f0  = bbar[i] - np.floor(bbar[i])           # 0.25
f   = row - np.floor(row)                   # [0.25, 0.75]
print(f"row = {row}, f0 = {f0}, f = {f}")

# Cut in (s1, s2): sum_j f_j s_j >= f0
# Substitute s = b - A x to express in original variables.
# coef on x_k  = -(f @ A[:, k])  ;   rhs in x-space = f @ b - f0
coef_x = -(f @ A)
rhs_x  = float(f @ b - f0)
print(f"Gomory cut in original variables:")
print(f"  {coef_x[0]:.2f} x1 + {coef_x[1]:.2f} x2 >= {rhs_x:.2f}")
# Flip sign to get a <= form (and clear fractions by 4):
# 4 x1 + 7 x2 <= 35
Convergence caveat Gomory's procedure converges in finitely many iterations if implemented carefully (lexicographic pivoting, integer arithmetic), but the bound is astronomical in the worst case. In practice pure Gomory cuts alone are too weak — solvers add hundreds of cuts of different families and rely on branching to close the gap.
II Mixed‑integer
Continuous non‑basics need a different formula.

The mixed‑integer Gomory cut.

When some variables are continuous, the pure fractional cut breaks — the key step "round down" fails for non-integers. The Mixed-Integer Gomory (MIG) cut repairs this by handling integer and continuous non-basics with separate, tight rules. Everything follows from one elementary observation about integers modulo a fractional gap.

Why pure Gomory stops working In Chapter I we relied on the fact that every term $\lfloor \bar a_{ij} \rfloor x_j$ is integer-valued. That is no longer true when some $x_j$ are continuous. MIG keeps the modular logic on the integer columns, but treats continuous columns by the only information that still matters for the forbidden gap: whether they push the row to the right or to the left.

Setup and notation

Suppose the simplex tableau contains a row for an integer-constrained basic variable $x_{B(i)}$ that has a fractional LP value $\bar b_i \notin \mathbb{Z}$:

\[ x_{B(i)} \;=\; \bar b_i \;-\; \sum_{j \in N} \bar a_{ij}\, x_j, \qquad x_{B(i)} \in \mathbb{Z}_+. \]

Define the fractional gap of the right-hand side:

\[ f_0 \;=\; \bar b_i - \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor \;\in\; (0,1). \]

Partition the non-basic index set $N = N_I \cup N_C$: $N_I$ = integer-constrained non-basics, $N_C$ = continuous (or free) non-basics. For each $j \in N_I$, split the tableau coefficient:

\[ \bar a_{ij} \;=\; \lfloor \bar a_{ij} \rfloor + f_{ij}, \qquad f_{ij} = \bar a_{ij} - \lfloor \bar a_{ij} \rfloor \in [0,1). \]

$f_{ij}$ is the fractional part of the $j$-th tableau coefficient.

Step 1 — Rewrite the row in terms of fractional parts

Substitute the split $\bar a_{ij} = \lfloor \bar a_{ij}\rfloor + f_{ij}$ into the tableau row:

\[ x_{B(i)} + \sum_{j \in N_I}\lfloor \bar a_{ij}\rfloor x_j + \sum_{j \in N_I} f_{ij} x_j + \sum_{j \in N_C} \bar a_{ij} x_j \;=\; \lfloor \bar b_i\rfloor + f_0. \]

Group all integer-valued pieces on the left. Since $x_{B(i)} \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $x_j \in \mathbb{Z}_+$ for $j \in N_I$, the quantity

\[ M \;:=\; x_{B(i)} + \sum_{j \in N_I}\lfloor \bar a_{ij}\rfloor x_j \]

is an integer. Subtract $M$ from both sides:

\[ \underbrace{\sum_{j \in N_I} f_{ij} x_j}_{\text{fractional part of int. non-basics}} + \underbrace{\sum_{j \in N_C} \bar a_{ij} x_j}_{\text{continuous non-basics}} \;=\; f_0 + K, \quad K := \lfloor \bar b_i\rfloor - M \in \mathbb{Z}. \]
Key equation The left-hand side equals an integer plus $f_0$. This is the crucial constraint we will exploit.

Step 2 — The forbidden gap

Let

\[ S \;:=\; \sum_{j \in N_I} f_{ij} x_j + \sum_{j \in N_C} \bar a_{ij} x_j. \]

The tableau row says $S = f_0 + K$ with $K \in \mathbb{Z}$. Because $f_0 \in (0,1)$, the values $f_0 + K$ skip the entire open interval $(f_0 - 1,\; f_0)$. So every integer-feasible point must fall into exactly one of two cases:

\[ S \;\le\; f_0 - 1 \qquad\text{or}\qquad S \;\ge\; f_0. \]

The current LP vertex has all non-basics equal to zero, so there $S = 0$. Since $0 \in (f_0 - 1,\; f_0)$, the LP point sits precisely inside the forbidden gap. That is the whole reason a separating cut exists.

Roadmap We now want a linear inequality $\sum_j \pi_j x_j \ge 1$ with two properties: it should evaluate to $0$ at the current LP vertex, and to at least $1$ at every integer-feasible point. The MIG coefficients are chosen so that touching either boundary of the forbidden gap contributes exactly one unit to the cut.

Step 3 — Match each variable to the nearest boundary

The cut we want has the form $\sum_j \pi_j x_j \ge 1$. The rule for $\pi_j$ is easier to remember than to derive formally: choose the coefficient so that moving exactly to a legal boundary contributes exactly one unit to the cut.

Coefficient rules

Integer non-basic $j \in N_I$ with $f_{ij} \le f_0$. One unit of $x_j$ moves $S$ to the right by $f_{ij}$. Since the nearest legal boundary on that side is $f_0$, the matching coefficient is

\[ \pi_j = \frac{f_{ij}}{f_0}. \]

Integer non-basic $j \in N_I$ with $f_{ij} > f_0$. One unit overshoots the right boundary. The useful quantity is therefore the complementary step $1-f_{ij}$ measured against the complementary gap $1-f_0$, which gives

\[ \pi_j = \frac{1-f_{ij}}{1-f_0}. \]

Continuous non-basic $j \in N_C$ with $\bar a_{ij} \ge 0$. A positive continuous column pushes $S$ to the right, so we scale it against the right boundary:

\[ \pi_j = \frac{\bar a_{ij}}{f_0}. \]

Continuous non-basic $j \in N_C$ with $\bar a_{ij} < 0$. A negative continuous column pushes $S$ to the left, so we scale it against the left boundary:

\[ \pi_j = \frac{-\bar a_{ij}}{1-f_0}. \]

For integer columns this piecewise rule is the famous tent function:

\[ \varphi_{f_0}(f) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{f}{f_0}, & 0 \le f \le f_0, \\[6pt] \dfrac{1-f}{1-f_0}, & f_0 < f < 1. \end{cases} \]

Three quick checks make the formula memorable. First, $\varphi_{f_0}(0)=0$: an already-integer coefficient contributes nothing. Second, $\varphi_{f_0}(f_0)=1$: one unit exactly reaches the boundary. Third, $\varphi_{f_0}(1^-)\to 0$: a coefficient whose fractional part is almost 1 is again nearly integer, so its effect should almost disappear.

Mixed‑integer Gomory cut With $f_0 = \bar b_i - \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor$ and $f_{ij} = \bar a_{ij} - \lfloor \bar a_{ij} \rfloor$, the inequality \[ \sum_{\substack{j \in N_I \\ f_{ij} \le f_0}} \frac{f_{ij}}{f_0} x_j + \sum_{\substack{j \in N_I \\ f_{ij} > f_0}} \frac{1-f_{ij}}{1-f_0} x_j + \sum_{\substack{j \in N_C \\ \bar a_{ij} \ge 0}} \frac{\bar a_{ij}}{f_0} x_j + \sum_{\substack{j \in N_C \\ \bar a_{ij} < 0}} \frac{-\bar a_{ij}}{1-f_0} x_j \;\ge\; 1 \] is valid for the mixed-integer set described by the tableau row. At the current LP basis all non-basics are zero, so its left-hand side is $0$ and the row is cut off immediately.
Disjunctive reading The row with $x_{B(i)} \in \mathbb{Z}$ implicitly contains the split disjunction $x_{B(i)} \le \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor$ or $x_{B(i)} \ge \lfloor \bar b_i \rfloor + 1$. The MIG cut is the strongest single linear inequality you get by projecting that split back into the non-basic variables. The tent and V-shape are the algebraic shadow of that two-branch picture.
Quick sanity check with f0 = 0.4 integer f = 0.2 -> coeff = 0.2 / 0.4 = 0.5 integer f = 0.4 -> coeff = 1.0 integer f = 0.8 -> coeff = (1 - 0.8) / (1 - 0.4) = 1/3 continuous a = 0.6 -> coeff = 0.6 / 0.4 = 1.5 continuous a = -0.3 -> coeff = 0.3 / 0.6 = 0.5

Coefficient shapes

The integer rule produces a tent; the continuous rule produces a V-shape. These graphs are worth studying once carefully: they turn the formula from something to memorize into something you can reconstruct on the fly.

What the two plots are really saying

The pictures compress the whole construction into one sentence: measure a variable by how efficiently it moves the row toward the nearest legal side of the forbidden gap. Integer columns have two ways to matter, hence the tent. Continuous columns only move left or right, hence the V-shape.

The peak value 1 is not arbitrary. It marks the column values for which a single unit of that variable lands exactly on a legal boundary. Values below the peak help, but do not finish the job; values above the peak overshoot and only their complementary part remains useful.

Why continuous columns use sign only A continuous variable has no modulo-1 behavior: it does not wrap around from fractional part 0.99 back to 0.01. So for $j \in N_C$ the only relevant question is whether increasing $x_j$ pushes $S$ right or left. That is why the rule depends on the sign of $\bar a_{ij}$, not on its fractional part.
Why tiny $f_0$ creates huge coefficients If $f_0$ is small, the LP row is already very close to the next integer on the right. A positive continuous coefficient then needs only a tiny movement in $S$ to cross the boundary, so the normalization by $1/f_0$ becomes large. That is not a bug: it reflects a row whose fractional basic value is extremely easy to separate.

A practical recipe

  1. Pick a tableau row whose basic variable is integer and fractional at the LP optimum.
  2. Compute $f_0 = \operatorname{frac}(\bar b_i)$.
  3. Classify each non-basic as integer or continuous, then apply the matching coefficient rule.
  4. Write the cut in non-basic space, then substitute slack definitions or tableau relations if you want it back in the original variables.

Worked example — MIG from a tableau row

Take the example from §I, but now treat $s_1, s_2$ as continuous. The tableau row for $x_1$ is:

\[ x_1 + \tfrac{9}{4} s_1 - \tfrac{1}{4} s_2 = \tfrac{9}{4}. \]

Here $\bar b_i = 9/4$, so $f_0 = 9/4 - 2 = 1/4$. Both slacks are continuous, which means we use the sign rule, not the tent rule.

  1. For $s_1$ we have $\bar a = 9/4 > 0$, so \[ \pi_{s_1} = \frac{\bar a}{f_0} = \frac{9/4}{1/4} = 9. \]
  2. For $s_2$ we have $\bar a = -1/4 < 0$, so \[ \pi_{s_2} = \frac{-\bar a}{1-f_0} = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \frac{1}{3}. \]
  3. The cut in slack space is therefore \[ 9s_1 + \tfrac{1}{3}s_2 \ge 1. \]

Now push it back to the original variables. Multiply by 3 to clear the fraction:

\[ 27 s_1 + s_2 \ge 3. \]

Substitute $s_1 = 6 - x_1 - x_2$ and $s_2 = 45 - 5x_1 - 9x_2$:

\[ 27(6 - x_1 - x_2) + (45 - 5x_1 - 9x_2) \ge 3. \]

Expand and collect terms:

\[ 207 - 32x_1 - 36x_2 \ge 3 \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad 32x_1 + 36x_2 \le 204. \]

Divide by 4:

\[ 8x_1 + 9x_2 \le 51. \]
Why the coefficient 9 is so large The fractional gap is only $f_0 = 1/4$, so the row is very close to the next integer boundary on the right. A positive continuous coefficient of size $9/4$ pushes hard in that direction, and the normalization by $f_0$ amplifies it to $9$.

At the LP optimum $(x_1, x_2) = (9/4,\; 15/4)$, the new inequality gives \[ 8\cdot \tfrac{9}{4} + 9\cdot \tfrac{15}{4} = 18 + \tfrac{135}{4} = 51.75 \;>\; 51, \] so the current vertex is cut off, exactly as it should be.

Python — MIG cut from one tableau row

import numpy as np

def mig_cut(row, b_i, integer_mask):
    """
    Build a Mixed-Integer Gomory cut from one tableau row.
      Tableau:  x_B(i) = b_i - sum_j row[j] * x_j
      Requires: x_B(i) integer, b_i fractional.

    Parameters
    ----------
    row           : array, non-basic coefficients (in simplex tableau sign)
    b_i           : float, fractional basic value
    integer_mask  : bool array, True iff x_j is integer-constrained

    Returns
    -------
    pi  : cut coefficients  (cut: pi @ x_N >= 1)
    """
    f0 = b_i - np.floor(b_i)
    assert 0 < f0 < 1, "b_i must be fractional"
    pi = np.zeros_like(row, dtype=float)
    for j, a in enumerate(row):
        if integer_mask[j]:
            f = a - np.floor(a)           # fractional part of tableau coeff
            if f <= f0:
                pi[j] = f / f0            # left arm of tent
            else:
                pi[j] = (1 - f) / (1 - f0)  # right arm of tent
        else:
            pi[j] = a / f0 if a >= 0 else -a / (1 - f0)
    return pi

# Demo: §I example, slacks continuous
row          = np.array([9/4, -1/4])  # coefficients of s1, s2 in tableau row
b_i          = 9/4
integer_mask = np.array([False, False])

pi = mig_cut(row, b_i, integer_mask)
print(f"f0 = {b_i - np.floor(b_i):.4f}")
print(f"MIG coefficients (s1, s2): {pi}")
print(f"Cut in slack space: {pi[0]:.4f}·s1 + {pi[1]:.4f}·s2 >= 1")
# Output:
#   f0 = 0.2500
#   MIG coefficients (s1, s2): [9.     0.333]
#   Cut in slack space: 9.0000·s1 + 0.3333·s2 >= 1

Solvers add MIG cuts in rounds: solve the LP, generate one MIG cut per fractional integer basic variable, re-solve, repeat until either no fractional basic remains or progress stalls. Then they branch.

III Rounding
A single inequality, mechanically tightened.

Mixed‑integer rounding.

MIG cuts arise from a particular row of the simplex tableau, but the same rounding logic applies to any linear combination of constraints — that combination need not be a tableau row at all. Distilling the principle gives the MIR inequality, which is the workhorse of generic cut generation.

From MIG to MIR MIG starts with one tableau row. MIR starts with any valid mixed-integer inequality. The logic is the same in both cases: the integer variables create a forbidden fractional gap, and the cut is the linear inequality that bridges across that gap. MIR is what remains after we strip away the simplex-specific language and keep only the rounding principle.

The base mixed set

Consider the elementary two‑variable set

\[ X^{\text{MIR}} \;=\; \{(y, s) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{R}_+ \;:\; y - s \le b\}. \]

Let $f = b - \lfloor b \rfloor \in (0,1)$.

How would we guess the cut? Suppose we look for a valid inequality of the form \[ y - \alpha s \le \lfloor b \rfloor. \] The cut should be tight at the first integer point above $\lfloor b \rfloor$, namely \[ (y,s) = (\lfloor b \rfloor + 1,\; 1-f), \] because that is exactly where the original constraint $y - s \le \lfloor b \rfloor + f$ meets the next integer level $y = \lfloor b \rfloor + 1$. Plugging this point into the target inequality gives \[ \lfloor b \rfloor + 1 - \alpha(1-f) = \lfloor b \rfloor, \] so \[ \alpha = \frac{1}{1-f}. \] This already explains where the mysterious denominator $1-f$ comes from.
The MIR inequality For every $(y,s) \in X^{\text{MIR}}$, \[ y - \tfrac{s}{1-f} \;\le\; \lfloor b \rfloor. \]

Proof. We have guessed the right candidate; now we only need to verify it. The proof splits cleanly on whether $y \le \lfloor b \rfloor$ or $y \ge \lfloor b \rfloor + 1$.

Case 1 — $y \le \lfloor b \rfloor$

Since $s \ge 0$ and $1-f > 0$, the term $s/(1-f)$ is nonneg. Therefore

\[ y - \frac{s}{1-f} \;\le\; y \;\le\; \lfloor b \rfloor. \]

The cut holds trivially. This case covers every integer $y$ that is already "small enough" — no rounding was needed.

Case 2 — $y \ge \lfloor b \rfloor + 1$

Write $y = \lfloor b \rfloor + k$ for some integer $k \ge 1$. From the original constraint $y - s \le b = \lfloor b \rfloor + f$:

\[ s \;\ge\; y - b \;=\; (\lfloor b \rfloor + k) - (\lfloor b \rfloor + f) \;=\; k - f. \]

Divide both sides by $1 - f > 0$:

\[ \frac{s}{1-f} \;\ge\; \frac{k - f}{1-f}. \]

Now check: is $\dfrac{k-f}{1-f} \ge k$? Equivalently, $k - f \ge k(1-f) = k - kf$. This holds iff $kf \ge f$, i.e.\ $k \ge 1$ — which is our assumption. Therefore

\[ \frac{s}{1-f} \;\ge\; k, \]

and thus

\[ y - \frac{s}{1-f} \;\le\; (\lfloor b \rfloor + k) - k \;=\; \lfloor b \rfloor. \]
Tightness — when does equality hold? In Case 2, equality $y - s/(1-f) = \lfloor b \rfloor$ holds when $s = k - f$ (i.e., the original constraint is tight) and $k = 1$ (i.e., $y = \lfloor b \rfloor + 1$). At that point $(y^*, s^*) = (\lfloor b \rfloor + 1,\; 1-f)$. The MIR cut is therefore a supporting hyperplane of the integer hull: it is tight at a genuine integer-feasible point, not just a weak bound.
Geometric reading The LP relaxation of $X^{\text{MIR}}$ is the halfspace below the line $y = b + s$ (slope 1 in the $s$–$y$ plane). The integer hull is the staircase "region below and to the right" of integer values of $y$. The MIR cut $y - s/(1-f) \le \lfloor b \rfloor$ defines a line with slope $1/(1-f) > 1$ — steeper than the LP boundary — passing exactly through the "corner" of the integer hull at $(s, y) = (1-f, \lfloor b \rfloor + 1)$. Fig. III.1 shows this directly.
One-line intuition If $y$ tries to jump from $\lfloor b \rfloor$ to $\lfloor b \rfloor + 1$, the slack variable must increase by at least $1-f$. The MIR cut simply charges exactly one unit for that minimum compensating increase.

The general MIR cut

Now consider any mixed‑integer inequality

\[ \sum_{j \in I} a_j y_j + \sum_{k \in C} g_k s_k \;\le\; b, \qquad y_j \in \mathbb{Z}_+,\; s_k \in \mathbb{R}_+. \]

Define $f_j = a_j - \lfloor a_j \rfloor$ and $f = b - \lfloor b \rfloor$, assume $f > 0$. The MIR inequality derived from this row is

General MIR cut \[ \sum_{j \in I} \Bigl(\lfloor a_j \rfloor + \tfrac{(f_j - f)^+}{1 - f}\Bigr) y_j \;+\; \sum_{\substack{k \in C \\ g_k < 0}} \tfrac{g_k}{1-f}\, s_k \;\le\; \lfloor b \rfloor, \] where $(t)^+ = \max(t, 0)$ and $f, f_j$ are the usual fractional parts.

Read the formula column by column:

  1. If an integer coefficient has fractional part $f_j \le f$, MIR leaves it at $\lfloor a_j \rfloor$.
  2. If an integer coefficient has fractional part $f_j > f$, MIR bumps it upward by $(f_j-f)/(1-f)$.
  3. If a continuous coefficient is nonnegative, MIR drops it from the cut.
  4. If a continuous coefficient is negative, MIR rescales it by the same factor $1/(1-f)$ that appeared in the base mixed set.
Why positive continuous columns disappear In a $\le$ row with $s_k \ge 0$, a positive continuous term $g_k s_k$ already pushes the left-hand side upward, so ignoring it yields a weaker inequality and therefore preserves validity. MIR keeps the smallest universally valid coefficient, which is 0.
Equivalence with MIG Applied to a simplex tableau row, the MIR cut is exactly the MIG cut after a change of variable. MIG is "MIR on a tableau row"; MIR is "MIG without the tableau". Once we have MIR, we can apply rounding to any aggregated inequality we like.

Aggregation and the c‑MIR procedure

The freedom of MIR is exploited by aggregating multiple rows of $Ax \le b$ with multipliers $\lambda \ge 0$, giving a combined inequality, complementing some bounded integer variables ($y_j \to u_j - y_j$), and scaling by some factor $\delta > 0$ before applying MIR. This is the complemented MIR (c‑MIR) separation heuristic — Marchand & Wolsey, 2001 — and is what commercial solvers actually compute under the "MIR cuts" banner.

c-MIR in solver language 1. aggregate rows to manufacture a promising mixed-integer inequality 2. complement bounded integer variables when that creates a better row fraction 3. scale the row, apply MIR, keep the cuts with the best violation
Why aggregation matters A raw model row may have a useless fractional part, or may mix the variables in a weak way. Aggregation lets the solver synthesize a row whose right-hand-side fraction and coefficient pattern are much more favorable for rounding. That is why "MIR cuts" in production are rarely taken from the model exactly as written.

Worked example — MIR on a single inequality

Start from

\[ 3y_1 + 2.5y_2 + 1.7y_3 - 0.4s_1 \le 6.3, \qquad y \in \mathbb{Z}_+^3,\; s_1 \ge 0. \]

Here $f = 6.3 - 6 = 0.3$. The fractional parts of the integer coefficients are

\[ f_1 = 0,\qquad f_2 = 0.5,\qquad f_3 = 0.7. \]
  1. $y_1$ is unchanged because $a_1 = 3$ is already integer.
  2. $y_2$ is bumped to $2 + (0.5 - 0.3)/0.7 = 2 + 2/7 = 16/7$.
  3. $y_3$ is bumped to $1 + (0.7 - 0.3)/0.7 = 1 + 4/7 = 11/7$.
  4. The continuous coefficient is negative, so it rescales to $-0.4/0.7 = -4/7$.
  5. The right-hand side rounds down from $6.3$ to $6$.

So the MIR cut is

\[ 3y_1 + \frac{16}{7}y_2 + \frac{11}{7}y_3 - \frac{4}{7}s_1 \le 6. \]

Multiplying through by 7 gives the cleaner equivalent form

\[ 21y_1 + 16y_2 + 11y_3 - 4s_1 \le 42. \] 
import numpy as np

def mir_cut(a, g, b):
    """
    MIR cut from   sum a_j y_j + sum g_k s_k  <=  b
    with  y integer >= 0,  s continuous >= 0.
    Returns (a_hat, g_hat, b_hat) such that
      sum a_hat[j] y_j + sum g_hat[k] s_k  <=  b_hat
    is valid for the mixed-integer set.
    """
    a = np.asarray(a, dtype=float)
    g = np.asarray(g, dtype=float)
    f = b - np.floor(b)
    if f == 0:
        return np.floor(a), np.minimum(g, 0.0), np.floor(b)   # trivial
    fj   = a - np.floor(a)
    bump = np.where(fj > f, (fj - f) / (1 - f), 0.0)
    a_hat = np.floor(a) + bump
    g_hat = np.where(g < 0, g / (1 - f), 0.0)                  # g_k >= 0 drop
    b_hat = np.floor(b)
    return a_hat, g_hat, b_hat


# 3 y1 + 2.5 y2 + 1.7 y3  -  0.4 s1  <=  6.3
a_hat, g_hat, b_hat = mir_cut(
    a=[3.0, 2.5, 1.7],
    g=[-0.4],
    b=6.3,
)
print("MIR cut coefficients on y :", np.round(a_hat, 6))
print("MIR cut coefficients on s :", g_hat)
print("MIR cut RHS               :", b_hat)
# Output:
#   MIR cut coefficients on y : [3.       2.285714 1.571429]
#   MIR cut coefficients on s : [-0.57142857]
#   MIR cut RHS               : 6.0
What changed, conceptually? The original row had two integer columns whose fractional parts were larger than the row fraction $f = 0.3$. Those are precisely the columns MIR strengthens. Everything else is either left alone or rescaled mechanically.
IV Knapsack
Cuts from a single 0/1 constraint.

Cover & knapsack cuts.

Gomory and MIR are generic — they ignore problem structure. The opposite extreme is to exploit a single combinatorial relation. The cleanest case is the 0/1 knapsack, where the entire family of facets has a beautifully simple description in terms of covers.

Let $a \in \mathbb{Z}_{++}^n$ and $b \in \mathbb{Z}_{++}$. The 0/1 knapsack set is

\[ X^{KP} = \Bigl\{ x \in \{0,1\}^n \;:\; \sum_{j=1}^n a_j x_j \le b \Bigr\}. \]
Cover A set $C \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}$ is a cover if $\sum_{j \in C} a_j > b$. It is minimal if no proper subset is itself a cover.
What the inequality is really saying A cover is a set of items that cannot all fit together. The cover cut \[ \sum_{j \in C} x_j \le |C|-1 \] does nothing more mysterious than forbid the impossible event "take every item in $C$ at once."

If $C$ is a cover then no feasible $x \in X^{KP}$ can have $x_j = 1$ for every $j \in C$ — that would exceed the capacity. So at most $|C| - 1$ of the variables in $C$ can be one.

Proof of validity. Suppose, for contradiction, $\sum_{j \in C} x_j \ge |C|$. Since $x \in \{0,1\}^n$, each $x_j \le 1$, so this forces $x_j = 1$ for every $j \in C$. But then $\sum_{j \in C} a_j x_j = \sum_{j \in C} a_j > b$, violating the knapsack constraint. ∎

Cover inequality For every cover $C$, \[ \sum_{j \in C} x_j \;\le\; |C| - 1 \] is valid for $X^{KP}$. If $C$ is minimal, the cover inequality is facet-defining for $\operatorname{conv}(X^{KP} \cap \{x_j = 0 : j \notin C\})$.
Why minimality matters A non-minimal cover $C$ contains a proper subset $C' \subset C$ that is already a cover. The cover inequality for $C'$ dominates: it involves fewer variables and is at least as tight. Solvers therefore always trim covers to minimality before adding them — it's a cheap post-processing step that can dramatically sharpen the cut.
From cover inequality to facet The cover inequality $\sum_{j \in C} x_j \le |C|-1$ involves only variables in $C$ — it says nothing about items outside $C$. This means it defines a facet only of a face of $\operatorname{conv}(X^{KP})$, not of the full polytope. To get a facet of the full polytope we need to assign positive coefficients to the "outside" variables, i.e., to lift the inequality (Chapter V). The lifted cover inequality is the dominant cutting tool in practice.

Separation

Given a fractional point $x^* \in [0,1]^n$, we want a cover whose inequality is violated. The violation of $\sum_{j \in C} x_j \le |C| - 1$ at $x^*$ can be written as

\[ \sum_{j \in C} x^*_j - (|C|-1) \;=\; |C| - \sum_{j \in C}(1 - x^*_j). \]

This is positive (the cut is violated) iff $\sum_{j \in C}(1-x^*_j) < 1$. So we seek a cover $C$ minimising $\sum_{j \in C}(1 - x^*_j)$ — equivalently, a set of items that forms a cover and has the maximum total $x^*$-value. This is a separation knapsack:

\[ \min \sum_{j=1}^n (1 - x^*_j)\, z_j \quad \text{s.t.}\;\; \sum_j a_j z_j \ge b + 1,\;\; z \in \{0,1\}^n. \]

If the minimum is $< 1$, the corresponding cover gives a violated cut. If $\ge 1$, no cover inequality cuts off $x^*$. The separation problem is NP-hard in general, but a greedy or LP-relaxed heuristic runs fast on practical instances.

How to read the separation objective high x*_j -> small penalty 1 - x*_j -> attractive item for the separator large a_j -> helps exceed capacity quickly good violated covers therefore combine heavy items that are already "almost on"

In the toy instance below, \[ a = (4,5,5,8,3),\qquad b=11,\qquad x^*=(0.6,0.7,0.8,0.2,0.5), \] the set $C = \{1,2,3\}$ is a natural candidate because $4+5+5=14>11$ and its penalty is \[ (1-0.6) + (1-0.7) + (1-0.8) = 0.4 + 0.3 + 0.2 = 0.9 < 1. \] Therefore the cover cut \[ x_1 + x_2 + x_3 \le 2 \] is violated at $x^*$ by \[ 0.6 + 0.7 + 0.8 - 2 = 0.1. \] This is exactly the kind of small but meaningful tightening that cover separators hunt for in practice.

Worked example — minimal cover separation

import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import linprog


def find_minimal_cover(a, b, x_star, eps=1e-6):
    """
    Heuristic separation of cover inequalities for
       sum_j a_j x_j <= b ,  x in {0,1}^n
    against the fractional point x_star.

    Strategy: solve the LP relaxation of the separation knapsack;
    round greedily; trim to a minimal cover.
    """
    n = len(a)
    c = (1 - np.asarray(x_star))           # minimize sum (1-x*) z
    # constraint:  -a^T z  <=  -(b+1)
    A_ub = np.array([[-ai for ai in a]])
    b_ub = np.array([-(b + 1.0)])
    res = linprog(c, A_ub=A_ub, b_ub=b_ub, bounds=(0, 1), method="highs")
    if not res.success:
        return None
    # Greedy round: pick variables in decreasing z, then by smallest (1-x*)
    order = sorted(range(n), key=lambda j: (-res.x[j], c[j]))
    C = []
    total = 0
    for j in order:
        C.append(j)
        total += a[j]
        if total > b:
            break
    if total <= b:
        return None                         # not a cover

    # Trim to minimal: try to drop the largest-a item that keeps it a cover
    changed = True
    while changed:
        changed = False
        for j in sorted(C, key=lambda j: -a[j]):
            if total - a[j] > b:
                C.remove(j); total -= a[j]
                changed = True
                break

    violation = sum(x_star[j] for j in C) - (len(C) - 1)
    return C, violation


# Demo: items with weights a and capacity b
a = [4, 5, 5, 8, 3]
b = 11
x_star = [0.6, 0.7, 0.8, 0.2, 0.5]
C, viol = find_minimal_cover(a, b, x_star)
print(f"minimal cover C = {C},  violation = {viol:.3f}")
# Cover inequality:  sum_{j in C} x_j  <=  |C| - 1
Generalised cover inequalities Plain cover inequalities only involve variables in $C$; they say nothing about $j \notin C$. To strengthen them into facets of $\operatorname{conv}(X^{KP})$ we need to assign coefficients to the variables outside $C$ — exactly the lifting problem of the next chapter.

Knapsack cuts more broadly

Beyond minimal cover inequalities, the literature has accumulated extended cover, $(1,k)$‑configuration, weight, and flow cover inequalities for variants like 0‑1 single‑node flow and bin packing. All of them apply to a single complicating constraint at a time and rely on the same machinery: identify a combinatorial obstacle, write down the inequality it implies, lift to obtain coefficients on the remaining variables.

V Lifting
Extending an inequality to ignored variables.

Lifting and extending.

Suppose we have a valid inequality for a restriction of $X$ where some variables are fixed at zero. Lifting recovers a valid inequality for the full $X$ by computing the largest coefficient those omitted variables can take without ever cutting off a feasible point.

A useful mindset Starting from an inequality on a restricted face does not mean the omitted variables should get coefficient 0. It means their coefficients have not been decided yet. Lifting is the process of discovering the largest values those coefficients can safely have.

Sequential up‑lifting

Let $X \subseteq \{0,1\}^n$, and suppose $\sum_{j \in S} \pi_j x_j \le \pi_0$ is valid for $X \cap \{x : x_k = 0\}$ — i.e., valid whenever the new variable $x_k$ is forced to zero. We want the largest coefficient $\pi_k$ such that the extended inequality still holds when $x_k = 1$. When $x_k = 1$ the constraint becomes $\sum_{j \in S} \pi_j x_j \le \pi_0 - \pi_k$, so we need

\[ \pi_0 - \pi_k \;\ge\; \max\Bigl\{ \sum_{j \in S} \pi_j x_j \;:\; x \in X,\; x_k = 1 \Bigr\}. \]

Rearranging: the lift coefficient on $x_k$ is

\[ \pi_k \;=\; \pi_0 \;-\; \max\Bigl\{ \sum_{j \in S} \pi_j x_j \;:\; x \in X,\; x_k = 1 \Bigr\}. \]

This is the tightest valid coefficient: any larger value would cut off the feasible point achieving the max. Setting $\pi_k$ exactly equal makes the lifted inequality a supporting hyperplane — it is tight at some feasible point, which is the prerequisite for being a facet.

If the inner max is $+\infty$ (no feasible $x$ has $x_k = 1$), then $\pi_k = -\infty$ and we can fix $x_k = 0$. Otherwise

\[ \sum_{j \in S} \pi_j x_j + \pi_k x_k \;\le\; \pi_0 \]

is valid for $X$.

Sequential lifting recipe 1. force the new variable x_k = 1 2. ask how large the old left-hand side can still become 3. subtract that maximum from the old right-hand side
Intuition: lifting = finding the right "penalty" Think of $\pi_k$ as the penalty the cut imposes when $x_k = 1$. If $x_k$ is a "heavy" item (large $a_k$), it leaves little room for cover items when it is selected, so the cut already has plenty of "tightness" to spare — $\pi_k$ can be large. If $x_k$ is "light" ($a_k$ small), cover items still fit alongside it, so the residual max is high and $\pi_k$ is small. The lift coefficient automatically encodes how much $x_k$ competes with the cover variables for capacity.
Order matters Sequential lifting adds variables one at a time; each lift uses the inequality already strengthened by all previous lifts. The lift coefficient therefore depends on the ordering. Different orderings yield different (all valid, all tight at some point) lifted inequalities — different facets of $\operatorname{conv}(X)$. In the worst case, exponentially many orderings give exponentially many facets; in practice a single "canonical" ordering (e.g., by decreasing $a_k$) produces strong cuts quickly.

Lifting a cover

Start from a minimal cover $C$ for the knapsack $\sum a_j x_j \le b$ and its inequality $\sum_{j \in C} x_j \le |C| - 1$. For each $k \notin C$, the lift problem reduces to a residual knapsack on the cover items:

\[ \pi_k = (|C|-1) - \max\Bigl\{ \sum_{j \in C} x_j \;:\; \sum_{j \in C} a_j x_j \le b - a_k,\; x \in \{0,1\}^{|C|} \Bigr\}. \]

The residual capacity is $b - a_k$: how much capacity remains after placing item $k$. If $a_k$ is large, little capacity is left, fewer cover items fit alongside $k$, the max is small, and $\pi_k$ is correspondingly large — item $k$ is genuinely "dangerous" and the cut penalises it heavily. If $a_k$ is small, cover items still pack in, $\pi_k$ may be 0 or 1, and $x_k$ barely affects the cut.

In words: $\pi_k$ measures how many cover items are forced out once item $k$ is chosen. If selecting $k$ leaves room for all the old cover items, then $\pi_k$ is small. If selecting $k$ crowds them all out, then $\pi_k$ is large.

Worked example — sequentially lifting a cover

import numpy as np
from itertools import product


def knapsack_max(values, weights, capacity):
    """Brute-force 0/1 knapsack maximum sum of values (small instances)."""
    n = len(values)
    best = 0
    if capacity < 0:
        return -10**9                       # infeasible: -infty
    for x in product([0, 1], repeat=n):
        if sum(w*xi for w, xi in zip(weights, x)) <= capacity:
            v = sum(v*xi for v, xi in zip(values, x))
            if v > best:
                best = v
    return best


def sequential_lift(a, b, cover, order):
    """
    Sequentially lift a cover inequality  sum_{j in cover} x_j <= |C|-1
    against the variables in `order` (a permutation of N \\ cover).
    Returns dict {j: pi_j} including cover (with pi_j = 1) and lifted vars.
    """
    pi   = {j: 1 for j in cover}
    rhs  = len(cover) - 1
    cap  = b

    cover_a   = [a[j] for j in cover]
    cover_idx = list(cover)

    for k in order:
        # Solve  max  sum pi_j x_j  s.t.  sum a_j x_j <= b - a[k],
        # over j in cover ∪ already-lifted vars.
        all_idx = cover_idx + [j for j in pi if j not in cover]
        vals = [pi[j] for j in all_idx]
        wts  = [a[j]  for j in all_idx]
        m = knapsack_max(vals, wts, b - a[k])
        pi[k] = rhs - m
        cover_idx = cover_idx                # (we keep order; cover unchanged)

    return pi, rhs


# --- demo ---
# knapsack: 4 x1 + 5 x2 + 5 x3 + 8 x4 + 3 x5  <=  11 ,   x binary
a = [4, 5, 5, 8, 3]
b = 11
cover = [0, 1, 2]                           # {x1, x2, x3} : 4+5+5 = 14 > 11
order = [3, 4]                              # lift x4 then x5

pi, rhs = sequential_lift(a, b, cover, order)
print("lifted cut:")
print("  ", " + ".join(f"{pi[j]} x{j+1}" for j in range(5)), "<=", rhs)
# Expected:  x1 + x2 + x3 + 2 x4 + 0 x5  <=  2

For this example the base cover inequality is \[ x_1 + x_2 + x_3 \le 2. \] Now lift the outside variables one by one.

  1. Lift $x_4$. If $x_4 = 1$, the residual capacity is $11 - 8 = 3$. None of $x_1,x_2,x_3$ fits into capacity 3, so the inner maximum is 0. Hence \[ \pi_4 = 2 - 0 = 2. \]
  2. Lift $x_5$ next. Now force $x_5 = 1$, so the residual capacity is $11 - 3 = 8$. In the already lifted system we maximize \[ x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + 2x_4 \] subject to the residual knapsack. The best choice is simply $x_4 = 1$, which already yields value 2 and uses all capacity 8. Therefore \[ \pi_5 = 2 - 2 = 0. \]
Resulting lifted cover cut The sequentially lifted inequality is \[ x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + 2x_4 + 0x_5 \le 2, \] usually written simply as \[ x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + 2x_4 \le 2. \] Item 4 is so heavy that choosing it wipes out the whole cover; item 5 is light enough that it adds no extra restriction in this lifting order.

Superadditive lifting

Solving a separate optimization problem for every lift coefficient is expensive. Superadditive lifting sidesteps this by constructing a single superadditive function $F : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ — superadditive means $F(u + v) \ge F(u) + F(v)$ — and setting $\pi_k = F(a_k)$. Constructing such an $F$ once, then evaluating at each $a_k$, replaces $n - |C|$ optimization problems with $n - |C|$ function evaluations. Solvers use this for production cut generation.

The classical superadditive function for minimal cover lifting (Balas, 1975) is piecewise linear, defined by the partial sums of cover weights. Working it out is a satisfying exercise — and a place where Gomory's machinery and combinatorial lifting visibly converge.

VI Closing
Cuts in the wild.

Putting it together.

A modern MILP solver runs a tightly choreographed dance of cuts and branches. Knowing what each family is tells you when to expect each to help — and when nothing short of branching will close the gap.

A typical cut loop

  1. Solve the LP relaxation.
  2. If integer-feasible, return. Otherwise collect the fractional integer-basic rows.
  3. Run separators in priority order: MIR (cheap and broad), MIG on each fractional integer basic, flow cover and GUB cover on structured constraints, knapsack cover on aggregated rows, clique on conflict graph, zero-half and Chvátal–Gomory on integer rows.
  4. Keep only cuts with violation above a threshold and not too parallel to existing cuts (angle filter). Add them.
  5. Re-solve, repeat for a bounded number of rounds.
  6. Branch on the most fractional variable.

The ordering of separators matters: MIR and MIG are run first because they are $O(m)$ per tableau row and reliably improve the LP bound on almost every problem. Cover separation is more expensive and problem-specific but can be dramatically better on capacitated problems.

Comparison of cut families

Family Source Cost Strength Best on
Gomory fractional Tableau row, all-integer O(n) Weak in practice Pure IPs; pedagogy
MIG (Mixed-int Gomory) Tableau row, mixed O(n) Good, universally applicable Any MILP with fractional basics
MIR Any aggregated row O(n) per row Strong; standard workhorse General MILPs; aggregated constraints
c-MIR Aggregated + complemented O(mn) Very strong; used in all solvers General MILPs
Cover 0/1 knapsack constraint NP-hard, heuristic O(n log n) Strong on capacitated problems Bin packing, scheduling, network flow
Lifted cover Cover + sequential lifting O(n) knapsacks per variable Facet-defining; state of art for 0/1 Knapsack, packing, GUB constraints
Chvátal–Gomory Any integer row combination Expensive separation Theoretically complete Cutting stock, pure integer programs
Split / LnP Split disjunction O(m²) LP per disjunction Theoretically complete (MILP) Deeply fractional, hard gap problems

Where each family shines

Rule of thumb MIR/c-MIR dominates wherever the LP relaxation has fractional aggregations of integer rows — essentially all general MILPs. Cover and lifted cover dominate on capacitated problems (knapsack, bin packing, fixed-charge networks) where a single combinatorial constraint is the bottleneck. Pure Gomory rarely beats MIG in practice; it survives only in pedagogy and convergence proofs. Split disjunctions are the heavy artillery for problems where the first few rounds of cheap cuts leave a large integrality gap.
Cuts are not free Each round of cut generation enlarges the LP and slows re-solve. Solvers therefore limit the number of rounds, the number of cuts per round, and use an orthogonality filter (keep only cuts sufficiently different from each other) to prevent the LP from becoming dense with near-duplicate cuts. The right balance between cutting and branching is problem-dependent and is one of the major tuning levers in modern solvers.

What we did not cover

This tour skipped several rich families: $\{0, \tfrac{1}{2}\}$-cuts and their generalization to mod-$k$ cuts (global, derived from parity structure); flow-cover and submodular cover inequalities for fixed-charge networks; intersection cuts derived from $S$-free convex sets (the unifying framework for all disjunctive cuts); clique and odd-hole inequalities from the conflict graph of binary variables (essential for machine scheduling and graph-colouring formulations).

The unifying thread is always the same: identify a polyhedral relaxation small enough to characterise completely, derive its facets, then project or lift back to the full variable space. Every cutting-plane result is, at heart, a statement about which halfspaces are needed to describe an integer hull.

One step further and we leave the world of explicit cuts altogether — extended formulations add new variables to express tight relaxations linearly, the column-generation viewpoint of cutting. But that is another chapter.
